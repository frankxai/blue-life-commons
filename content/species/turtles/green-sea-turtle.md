# Green Sea Turtle (*Chelonia mydas*)

The green sea turtle is a large species of sea turtle found throughout tropical and subtropical seas around the world, with major nesting populations in the Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian Oceans.

## Taxonomy and description

Green sea turtles are the only herbivorous sea turtles. They derive their common name from the greenish color of their subdermal fat rather than their shell. Their carapace (shell) is heart-shaped and ranges in color from olive to brown and black. Adults can weigh between 110 and 190 kilograms (240 to 420 pounds) and have a shell length of up to 1.2 meters (4 feet).

## Habitat and life history

Green sea turtles utilize different habitats throughout their lives. Hatchlings enter the open ocean (pelagic phase) where they feed on plankton. As juveniles, they migrate to coastal seagrass beds and coral reefs (neritic phase) and transition to a herbivorous diet consisting primarily of seagrasses and algae. 

Nesting females migrate long distances back to their natal beaches to deposit clutches of eggs in sandy nests.

## Conservation status

The IUCN Red List classifies the green sea turtle globally as **Endangered**. Despite recovery in some protected regions, nesting populations continue to decline overall.

Major threats include:
- Harvesting of eggs and adults for food and materials
- Destruction of nesting beach habitats due to coastal development and artificial lighting
- Bycatch in commercial gillnets, trawls, and longlines
- Marine debris ingestion (particularly plastics resembling jellyfish)
- Fibropapillomatosis, a disease causing debilitating tumors
