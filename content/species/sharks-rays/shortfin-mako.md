## Identification

The shortfin mako is a large, highly migratory shark with a pointed (conical) snout, light metallic-blue sides, and a white underside. It has relatively small eyes and pectoral fins compared with the closely related longfin mako (*Isurus paucus*) ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/shortfin-mako-shark)).

## Ecology and behavior

The shortfin mako occurs across all temperate and tropical ocean waters and ranges widely across the open ocean. It is a member of the mackerel shark family (Lamnidae), the group that includes regional endothermy ("warm-bodied" physiology). The species is reported to be among the fastest-swimming sharks; specific speed, diet, and movement claims should be cited to published research and confirmed in review ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/shortfin-mako-shark)).

## Conservation status and threats

The IUCN Red List assesses the shortfin mako globally as **Endangered** (assessed 2018, published in version 2019-1), with a **decreasing** population trend. The global assessment inferred a median decline of roughly 47% over three generations, with much steeper regional declines — at least 80% over three generations inferred for the Mediterranean. The dominant pressure is incidental capture (and retention) in pelagic fisheries, particularly longlines and driftnets ([IUCN Red List](https://www.iucnredlist.org/species/39341/2903170)).

Documented threats include:

- Bycatch and targeted retention in high-seas pelagic longline and driftnet fisheries
- High value of fins and meat
- Slow life history that limits recovery from fishing mortality

The species was added to **CITES Appendix II** in 2019, requiring trade permits and monitoring ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/shortfin-mako-shark)). In November 2022, NOAA Fisheries determined that the shortfin mako does not warrant listing under the U.S. Endangered Species Act ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/shortfin-mako-shark)). Report figures as the cited authorities state them; a reviewer should confirm the current assessment and regional management.

## How to observe responsibly

**This page does not provide approach guidance.** As an open-ocean species, the shortfin mako is rarely encountered deliberately except in fishing or pelagic-dive contexts. Any encounter must follow a reviewed observation guide and local regulations — distance first, no feeding, baiting, chasing, or surrounding.
