# Staghorn Coral (*Acropora cervicornis*)

Staghorn coral is a branching stony coral found in shallow reefs throughout the Caribbean and warm waters of the western Atlantic. It is one of the most important reef-building corals in the region.

## Taxonomy and description

Staghorn coral belongs to the family Acroporidae. It is named for its cylindrical, branching structure that resembles stag antlers. The branches grow rapidly, up to 10 to 20 centimeters (4 to 8 inches) per year, and range in color from golden-brown to tan, with white tips where new growth occurs.

## Ecological role and habitat

Staghorn coral grows in shallow reef zones, typically at depths of 1 to 20 meters (3 to 65 feet). It creates dense, complex branching thickets known as "haystacks" that provide critical three-dimensional habitat, shelter, and nursery grounds for a wide variety of reef fish, lobsters, and other marine organisms.

## Conservation status

The IUCN Red List classifies the staghorn coral globally as **Critically Endangered** due to massive population declines (over 80-90% since the 1980s).

Primary threats include:
- Coral bleaching driven by rising sea surface temperatures (associated with climate change)
- Disease outbreaks, particularly White Band Disease, which decimated large stands of staghorn coral
- Ocean acidification reducing calcification rates and weakening the coral skeleton
- Localized impacts, such as runoff, sediment pollution, and physical damage from anchors or storms
