# Orca / Killer Whale (*Orcinus orca*) — Focus: Southern Resident Population

**IUCN Status:** Data Deficient (DD) — insufficient data for global assessment  
**Southern Resident Killer Whale (SRKW) — US ESA Status:** Endangered  
**SRKW population (2026):** ~73 individuals (J, K, and L pods)  
**Canada SARA Status:** Endangered

---

## The Species and the Population

*Orcinus orca* is the most cosmopolitan cetacean on Earth — found in every ocean from the Arctic to the Antarctic, with a global population that may number in the tens of thousands. The IUCN classifies it as Data Deficient because no reliable global estimate exists. This page focuses specifically on the **Southern Resident Killer Whales (SRKWs)**, the most endangered population of orcas in the world, whose range centers on the waters of Puget Sound, the Salish Sea, and the coast of Washington, Oregon, and California.

These 73 animals are among the most intensively studied large mammals on the planet. The Center for Whale Research (CWR) has conducted an unbroken annual census since 1976 — one of the longest continuous population studies of any wild marine mammal. Every individual is known by name, pod membership, family lineage, and reproductive history.

---

## A Species, Not a Single Animal

Before understanding the SRKWs' predicament, it is essential to understand that "killer whale" does not describe a single behavioral or ecological type. Orcas are more accurately described as an umbrella for several distinct populations — some argue distinct species or subspecies — that have evolved radically different ecologies, cultures, and even physical forms.

In the Northeast Pacific alone, three named ecotypes exist:

**Residents** are fish-eating, socially stable, highly vocal specialists. They live in stable matrilineal groups and transmit foraging and behavioral knowledge across generations. Males stay with their mothers for life — one of only a handful of mammal species where this is the rule, not the exception. Southern Residents are the southernmost resident population.

**Transients (Bigg's killer whales)** are marine-mammal hunters — seals, porpoises, sea lions. They are typically quieter while hunting, travel in smaller groups, and overlap geographically with Residents without interacting. They do not interbreed with Residents.

**Offshores** are an open-ocean population that feeds primarily on sharks. Their teeth show characteristic shark-scale wear. They are the least studied ecotype.

These three groups have distinct dialects, genomes, and social structures. Residents and Transients have not interbred in an estimated 100,000–700,000 years. The ecological and cultural divergence between ecotypes rivals the divergence between closely related but separate mammal species.

## The Southern Resident Crisis

The SRKWs have declined from 98 individuals in 1995 to approximately 73 in 2026. The population dips below replacement with regularity. Understanding why requires understanding what they eat.

**Southern Residents are Chinook salmon specialists.** Roughly 95% of their prey, by biomass, is Chinook salmon (*Oncorhynchus tshawytscha*) — the largest Pacific salmon species, with the highest fat content. This is not a dietary preference in the casual sense: it is a deeply embedded cultural specialization. Resident killer whales learn their prey preference from their mothers. Calves observe, practice, and inherit foraging strategies across generations. When Chinook become scarce, Southern Residents do not switch prey. The behavioral infrastructure for switching simply does not exist within this culture.

Chinook salmon populations throughout the Columbia River system — the SRKWs' primary prey source — have declined dramatically over the past century. The Columbia and its tributaries are the most dammed river system in the world. Four dams on the lower Snake River in particular — Ice Harbor, Lower Monumental, Little Goose, and Lower Granite — block access to some of the highest-quality Chinook habitat remaining in the Pacific Northwest. A multi-year, multi-agency federal analysis (completed 2024) found that breaching these four dams would be the single most effective recovery action for both Snake River Chinook and Southern Resident orcas. The political and economic dimensions of that decision — particularly the hydroelectric power those dams generate and the agricultural irrigation they support — have prevented action.

## Threats (Ranked by Impact)

### 1. Prey Depletion — Chinook Salmon
This is the primary driver of SRKW decline. Below-average Chinook returns correlate directly with poor SRKW reproductive success and increased mortality. Undernourished females have reduced pregnancy rates; undernourished pregnant females experience high rates of late-term pregnancy failure (documented by prey-stress biomarkers in fecal samples). Calves from malnourished mothers have higher first-year mortality. The population's reproductive ceiling is set by salmon abundance.

### 2. Vessel Noise and Disturbance
Southern Residents hunt by echolocation — they emit high-frequency clicks and interpret the returning echoes to locate, track, and capture individual salmon. The broadband noise produced by vessel engines, particularly in the 1–10 kHz range where SRKW sonar operates, directly degrades their ability to detect and catch prey. Studies using acoustic tags and hydrophone arrays have demonstrated that SRKWs in the presence of vessels reduce echolocation click rates, vocalize less, and consume fewer fish per hour. Under the current legal framework, vessels must maintain a 200-meter standoff (US) or 400-meter standoff (Canada) — but hundreds of recreational and commercial vessels transit SRKW core habitat daily in summer, creating a persistent noise environment even when no single vessel is technically in violation.

### 3. PCB Contamination
Southern Residents carry the highest documented PCB loads of any cetacean population on Earth. PCBs (polychlorinated biphenyls) are legacy industrial compounds banned in North America in the late 1970s but persistent in marine sediments and food webs. They bioaccumulate in fatty tissues and biomagnify up the food chain: Chinook salmon carry high PCB loads, and SRKWs consuming thousands of kilograms of salmon per year accumulate extreme concentrations. PCBs suppress immune function, disrupt reproduction, and reduce calf survival. They are transmitted from mother to calf through fat-rich milk; firstborn calves typically receive the highest doses. There is no feasible remediation pathway for PCBs already in the whales' bodies — the exposure pathway (salmon) must be controlled at the sediment and source level.

### 4. Oil Spill Risk
The Trans Mountain Pipeline Expansion (TMX), completed in 2024, tripled the capacity of oil transport through the Salish Sea — the SRKWs' core habitat. A major spill in the Salish Sea during summer foraging would be catastrophic: SRKWs have no behavioral mechanism to avoid surface oil slicks, their prey would be contaminated, and cleanup operations would generate enormous noise disturbance. The TMX approval process was contested by SRKW conservation advocates; the expanded oil tanker traffic represents a structural increase in spill risk to the population.

## The Cultural Dimension

Among biologists and legal scholars, the Southern Residents have become a focal point for discussions about cetacean culture and whether highly cultural, cognitively complex animals deserve legal frameworks that go beyond species-level protection. The SRKWs have distinct dialects within each pod (J, K, L pods each have recognizable call repertoires); these dialects shift slowly over time through cultural transmission, not genetic change. They engage in behaviors — fish-sharing, play, coordinated hunting, apparent grieving — that reflect sophisticated social cognition.

Tahlah Mäkinen, a calf born to J pod in 2020, was celebrated across the Pacific Northwest with the same media coverage given to human births. The public grief when calves die — or when a mother like Tahlah's grandmother J35 was documented carrying her dead calf for 17 days in 2018 — reflects a recognition, difficult to articulate within standard conservation frameworks, that what is being lost with each SRKW death is not merely a unit of genetic diversity but a bearer of culture.

## Conservation Status and Active Measures

NOAA's Recovery Plan for Southern Resident Killer Whales (2008, updated periodically) identifies three threat categories — prey availability, vessel effects, and contaminants — and establishes recovery criteria (a population of at least 95 individuals over 28 years). Current population trajectory does not approach that target.

Active measures include:
- **Vessel approach regulations:** 200 meters in US waters (NOAA rule, 2021); 400 meters in Canadian waters (Fisheries Act regulations). Power-down requirements within 400 meters in Canada.
- **Be Whale Wise guidelines:** A voluntary code of conduct for whale-watch operators, widely adopted but not universally followed.
- **Snake River dam analysis:** Federal environmental review process (2024) examining dam removal options; decision pending.
- **Fisheries adjustments:** NOAA has implemented Chinook salmon harvest reductions in areas where SRKW prey on salmon, in coordination with tribal co-managers under treaty obligations.
- **PCB remediation efforts:** Limited sediment cleanup projects in Commencement Bay (Tacoma) and other contaminated Salish Sea sites.

## What Each Persona Can Do

**NGOs:** The highest-leverage advocacy target is Snake River dam removal — specifically the four lower Snake River dams. Supporting the tribal nations (particularly the Nez Perce, Umatilla, Warm Springs, and Yakama tribes) who have advocated for dam removal as both a salmon recovery measure and a treaty rights issue is essential context. Vessel noise regulations — pushing for mandatory quiet zones and stricter enforcement of existing standoff rules — are the near-term, achievable intervention.

**Researchers:** High-priority research needs include: improved methods for measuring individual prey intake in free-ranging whales (acoustic tag approaches are advancing); long-term PCB depuration modeling under different salmon exposure scenarios; behavioral studies of SRKW response to different vessel classes and speeds; and population viability modeling that integrates prey, noise, and contaminant stressors simultaneously.

**Creators:** J pod has known individuals, family histories, and personalities documented across 50 years of research. Granny (J2), the matriarch who died in 2016 estimated at over 100 years old, and Tahlah's family line are narrative handles for communicating whale culture to general audiences. The 50 years of Center for Whale Research photo-ID data is publicly accessible at orca.org — a resource of extraordinary richness for data visualization and storytelling.

**Communities:** Reporting SRKW sightings to the Orca Network (orcanetwork.org) contributes to real-time population monitoring. Recreational boaters in the Salish Sea can exceed minimum standoff distances voluntarily. Supporting local watershed restoration — riparian habitat, culvert removal, spawning gravel rehabilitation — directly supports Chinook recovery and, downstream, SRKW prey availability.
