# Ocean Literacy 101: Ten Things Every Person Should Know

The ocean is the defining feature of this planet. It covers 71% of Earth's surface, contains 97% of Earth's water, and produces more than half of the oxygen we breathe. Yet most people know more about the surface of the moon than the floor of the ocean.

These ten principles are the minimum baseline for ocean literacy — the knowledge that grounds every decision about how we treat the systems that keep us alive.

---

## 1. The ocean regulates our climate

The ocean absorbs more than 90% of the excess heat trapped by greenhouse gases. Without it, Earth's atmosphere would be roughly 36°C hotter than it is today. The ocean also absorbs about 30% of all CO₂ emissions, slowing — but not stopping — atmospheric warming. The cost of this service: warmer, more acidic water that is increasingly hostile to marine life.

Current state: ocean surface temperature is now +1.1°C above the pre-industrial baseline (2026 measurement). Rate of warming: +0.17°C per decade, accelerating.

---

## 2. The ocean is acidifying

When CO₂ dissolves in seawater it forms carbonic acid. Ocean pH has dropped from 8.18 pre-industrially to 8.08 today — a 26% increase in acidity on the logarithmic scale. Rate: −0.0017 pH units per year.

Why it matters: marine organisms that build shells from calcium carbonate — corals, oysters, pteropods, sea urchins — cannot calcify normally as pH falls. Below aragonite saturation (expected in some regions by the 2050s under current trajectories), their shells dissolve faster than they form.

---

## 3. The ocean feeds 3 billion people

Seafood is the primary protein source for 3.3 billion people globally, concentrated in coastal developing nations with limited alternatives. Marine fisheries support the livelihoods of 600 million people, most of them small-scale. The ocean is not a luxury resource — for a significant portion of humanity, it is a food security infrastructure.

---

## 4. Coral reefs are in crisis

Coral reefs cover less than 0.2% of the ocean floor but support approximately 25% of all marine species. They have lost more than 50% of their total coverage since 1950. The primary driver is thermal bleaching: when water temperature exceeds the coral's tolerance by just 1°C for eight or more weeks, the symbiotic algae that feed the coral are expelled and the coral starves.

At +1.5°C of global warming (expected before 2040 under current trajectories), 70–90% of remaining reefs bleach annually. At +2.0°C, functional reef ecosystems effectively cease to exist.

---

## 5. Only 8% of the ocean is protected

As of 2026, 8.1% of the global ocean falls within a marine protected area — of which only 2.7% is fully or highly protected. The international community committed to protecting 30% by 2030 (CBD Kunming-Montreal Target 3). At current rates of designation, we will not reach that target.

Well-designed marine reserves with full protection generate measurable recovery: fish biomass inside fully protected MPAs averages 670% higher than in adjacent fished areas within 10 years.

---

## 6. Plastic pollution is permanent at human timescales

An estimated 8–12 million tonnes of plastic enter the ocean every year. Most plastic does not biodegrade — it fragments into smaller and smaller pieces, down to nanoplastics that have been detected in human blood, breast milk, and the placentas of newborns. There are an estimated 5.25 trillion plastic pieces floating in the ocean today.

No technology currently exists to collect microplastics from the open ocean. The only effective response is preventing plastic from entering in the first place.

---

## 7. The deep ocean is largely unexplored

Only about 20% of the ocean floor has been mapped to the resolution we use for Mars. The deep ocean — below 200 m — represents 95% of Earth's livable volume and contains ecosystems we have barely begun to understand. Hydrothermal vent communities, cold seep ecosystems, and abyssal plains harbour unique life found nowhere else. This is the largest biome on Earth. We do not know what we are losing.

---

## 8. Marine oxygen comes from the ocean

More than 50% of the oxygen in Earth's atmosphere is produced by marine phytoplankton — microscopic plant-like organisms that drift in the sunlit upper ocean. A single genus, *Prochlorococcus*, produces approximately 20% of the oxygen in every breath you take. Warming, acidification, and nutrient changes are altering phytoplankton community composition in ways that are not yet fully understood.

---

## 9. Ocean dead zones are expanding

There are over 700 documented hypoxic (oxygen-depleted) zones in the world's coastal waters, covering approximately 700,000 km². They form where excess nutrients — primarily from agricultural runoff — trigger algal blooms that deplete oxygen when they decompose. Fish, shrimp, and mobile invertebrates flee; sessile animals die. Dead zones are expanding at approximately 2% per year.

---

## 10. What you do on land reaches the ocean

Every river drains to the sea. Fertiliser applied in the American Midwest creates dead zones in the Gulf of Mexico. Plastic discarded in a landlocked city eventually reaches the ocean via rivers and stormwater. Air pollution from power plants drives ocean acidification. The ocean is not a separate system — it is the terminal recipient of everything that washes off the land.
