# Artificial Reef Design: Materials, Structure, and Biodiversity Outcomes

## Purpose and ecological rationale

Artificial reefs are purpose-deployed or repurposed hard structures placed on the seabed to provide habitat complexity in areas where natural reef has been destroyed or was never present. The ecological value of an artificial reef derives from the same principle that underlies natural reef function: hard, structurally complex substrate in an otherwise featureless sandy or muddy seabed concentrates biodiversity by offering settlement surfaces for invertebrates, refugia for fish, and attachment points for algae and corals that form the base of the food web.

The primary ecological and conservation purposes of artificial reefs are:

- **Substitute hard substrate** in areas where natural reef has been degraded by bleaching, physical damage, or crown-of-thorns starfish outbreaks, and where environmental conditions still support reef community recovery if suitable substrate is available
- **Increase fish biomass and species richness** at a deployment site — artificial reefs consistently show higher fish density and diversity compared to surrounding bare substrate within 1–3 years of deployment
- **Physical exclusion of bottom trawling** — the presence of a hard relief structure on the seabed prevents trawl nets from being dragged over the area, providing de facto protection to the seabed community around the reef footprint even in the absence of formal protected area designation
- **Carbon sequestration via calcareous organisms** — barnacles, oysters, tube worms, hard corals, and coralline algae all build calcium carbonate structures that represent long-term carbon storage; well-colonised artificial reefs accumulate substantial carbonate biomass over decades

## Design principles

The difference between an artificial reef that becomes a thriving ecosystem and one that remains a barren concrete block comes down primarily to structural design.

### Surface complexity and rugosity

**Rugosity** — a dimensionless index measuring the ratio of actual surface area to planar footprint — is the single most predictive metric of a reef structure's biodiversity potential. Flat concrete slabs have a rugosity index near 1.0. Natural coral reefs typically measure 2.0–4.0. Purpose-built artificial reef modules should target a **rugosity index >1.5** as a minimum design criterion.

Rugosity is created by:
- Surface texture (pitting, granularity, micro-channels) that creates settlement microhabitats for larval invertebrates and coralline algae
- Macro-scale overhangs, ledges, and caves that provide shade and refugia for territorial reef fish
- Crevice density — the number of openings per unit surface that allow small fish and invertebrates to shelter from predators

Research consistently shows that fish species richness saturates at a rugosity of approximately 2.0–2.5; beyond that threshold, diversity gains are diminishing while structural costs increase.

### Stability under storm surge

Any structure placed on the seabed must be designed to resist displacement under worst-case current and wave conditions for its deployed depth and location. Structures that are overturned or displaced damage surrounding habitat and lose colonisation progress. Design standards reference significant wave height at the deployment site plus a safety factor; mass and footprint geometry must be sufficient to resist calculated hydrodynamic forces.

### Material selection

Material selection is one of the most consequential decisions in artificial reef design and has been the source of the most documented failures.

**Concrete (purpose-built modules):** Marine-grade concrete is the most widely recommended material for purpose-built artificial reefs. It is chemically stable in seawater, structurally durable for decades, and provides a suitable pH environment for coral settlement (concrete's carbonate chemistry is more compatible with coral settlement than inert materials such as steel or plastic). Concrete can be cast into complex geometries — including internal void structures that natural corals cannot provide at equivalent density.

**Scuttled vessels:** Decommissioned ships are the largest-footprint artificial reef structures typically deployed. When properly prepared, they provide extraordinary biodiversity value — the three-dimensional complexity of a ship hull, superstructure, and internal compartments creates a diversity of microhabitats at scales unavailable in module systems. However, scuttling requires rigorous **decontamination protocols** to remove fuel, hydraulic fluid, lubricants, coolant (PCBs), insulation materials (asbestos, foam), anti-fouling paint (TBT or copper-based), and any hazardous cargo residue. Incomplete decontamination results in a toxic artificial reef. Regulatory requirements for scuttling vary by jurisdiction; in most nations, environmental permits require a full hazardous materials survey and remediation report before sinking is approved.

**Materials to avoid:** The historical record includes a number of failed artificial reef deployments that should not be replicated:

- **Tyres:** Deployed at scale in the 1970s and 1980s (notably the Osborne Reef off Fort Lauderdale — approximately 2 million tyres), tyres proved to be ecologically useless artificial reefs. They do not provide stable colonisation surface (rubber is not compatible with larval settlement), they disperse in storms (causing abrasion damage to natural reefs), and they leach zinc and other compounds over time. The Osborne Reef was declared an environmental catastrophe; cleanup efforts to recover and recycle the tyres have been ongoing since 2007 at substantial public expense.
- **Metal drums, PVC pipes, and industrial waste materials:** Any material that degrades, leaches chemicals, or lacks structural permanence should be excluded.

## The 3D printing revolution

The most significant recent innovation in artificial reef design is the application of large-format 3D printing to produce reef modules with surface complexity that was previously impossible or prohibitively expensive to manufacture.

### Reef Design Lab

Melbourne-based Reef Design Lab has pioneered the design of artificial reef modules that replicate the micro-topography of natural *Porites* and *Acropora* corals using parametric design software and ceramic/concrete printing technologies. Their modules incorporate overhangs, micro-channels, and pore networks at scales (1–50 mm) that provide larval settlement microhabitats for corals, sponges, and encrusting invertebrates.

### Archireef

Hong Kong-based Archireef, founded in 2019, produces terracotta-ceramic reef tiles ("Reef Tiles") using robotic 3D printing. Terracotta is selected for its pH compatibility with coral settlement chemistry and its granular surface texture, which retains larval settlement cues. Each tile is 0.4 m², printed with a surface pattern derived from computed scans of living *Porites* corals, and designed to stack and interlock without adhesive bonding.

As of 2026, Archireef has deployed over **1,000 units** across test sites in Hong Kong (Hoi Ha Wan Marine Park), the UAE (Abu Dhabi, Ras Al Khaimah), and several Southeast Asian reef restoration sites. Preliminary monitoring data from the Hong Kong deployments, covering the first 18–24 months, shows approximately **26% faster coral settlement rates** on the Reef Tile surfaces compared to plain flat concrete control panels at the same sites. Fish species richness at the Reef Tile deployments exceeded control panels within 12 months.

## Scuttled vessel case studies

### SS Thistlegorm, Red Sea, Egypt

The SS Thistlegorm, a British armed merchant vessel sunk in 1941 by German aircraft, lies at approximately 30 m depth in the northern Red Sea near Sharm el-Sheikh. After its rediscovery by Jacques Cousteau in the 1950s and subsequent popularisation as a dive site, it has been continuously monitored as an unintentional long-term study in artificial reef ecological succession. Over 80 years of colonisation, the wreck hosts biological diversity comparable to nearby natural reef sections, including permanent populations of batfish, lionfish, moray eels, and, in recent decades, several species of hard coral that have established on the hull exterior. The Thistlegorm is the most frequently cited evidence that scuttled steel vessels can achieve natural reef-equivalent biodiversity given sufficient time and site conditions.

### USS Oriskany, Florida, USA

The USS Oriskany, a decommissioned aircraft carrier, was sunk in May 2006 approximately 38 km south of Pensacola, Florida, as part of the Florida Fish and Wildlife Conservation Commission's artificial reef programme. At 274 m length, it is the largest purpose-sunk artificial reef vessel in the world. The Oriskany was extensively decontaminated before sinking — a three-year process that removed asbestos, fuel, and hazardous materials from the 32,000-tonne vessel. Post-deployment surveys have documented rapid colonisation: within three years, the wreck hosted over 140 fish species, and the vertical hull faces have developed significant encrusting coral and sponge communities. The Oriskany is now considered one of the most successful artificial reef deployments in the United States and is a reference case for decontamination protocol standards.

## Biodiversity metrics to monitor

A rigorous artificial reef monitoring programme tracks the following metrics at baseline (pre-deployment) and at regular post-deployment intervals (typically 1, 3, 5, and 10 years):

- **Fish species richness** — number of distinct species recorded per standardised survey unit (usually belt transect or roving diver count); target is to approach or exceed the regional natural reef benchmark
- **Fish biomass per m²** — total estimated mass of fish per unit survey area; higher biomass indicates trophic productivity and food web development
- **Coral cover %** — percentage of hard substrate surface covered by living stony coral tissue; particularly important for reefs intended to serve as coral restoration platforms
- **Invertebrate diversity index** — presence and abundance of key taxonomic groups (echinoderms, crustaceans, molluscs, polychaetes, sponges) as a proxy for ecosystem function

## Timeline expectations

Colonisation of artificial reefs follows a broadly predictable ecological succession pattern, though the exact timeline varies with depth, water quality, larval availability, and structural complexity:

**Year 1 — pioneer colonisers:** The first year is dominated by fast-settling opportunist species. Encrusting algae and coralline algae colonise bare surfaces within weeks to months. Filter-feeding invertebrates (barnacles, tube worms, oysters, and tunicates) establish. Small crustaceans (amphipods, copepods, small crabs) occupy crevices. Soft corals (gorgonians, alcyonarians) begin settling on shaded surfaces. Small schooling fish (wrasse, damselfish) begin using the structure for orientation and shelter.

**Years 3–5 — territorial reef fish establishing:** By year 3, larger demersal fish species (grouper, snapper, moray eels) establish territorial home ranges on the structure. Predator-prey dynamics begin to emerge. Hard coral colonies begun in year 1–2 are now measurable (2–10 cm diameter). The structure begins to function as a refugium and feeding station for fish from the surrounding habitat, even if the reef itself is not yet fully functional as a breeding habitat.

**Year 10+ — structural maturity and apex predator use:** Coral colonies reaching 10–20 cm diameter begin contributing structural complexity. Apex predators (sharks, large groupers, rays) make regular visits or establish residency. In well-sited reefs with good larval connectivity, species richness may approach 60–80% of regional natural reef benchmarks. Carbonate accumulation from calcareous organisms becomes measurable as a structural contribution.
