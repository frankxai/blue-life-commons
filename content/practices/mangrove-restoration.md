# Mangrove Restoration

## Global status

Mangrove forests — the intertidal woody ecosystems that bridge land and sea in tropical and subtropical coastlines — have lost approximately **35% of their global extent since 1970**. Approximately 14.5 million hectares remain, covering roughly 13% of the world's coastlines. Losses have been driven primarily by conversion to shrimp aquaculture ponds, rice paddies, and coastal development. Deforestation rates have slowed from historical peaks but remain a concern in Southeast Asia, West Africa, and parts of the Americas.

## Why mangroves matter

Mangroves provide ecological services at an intensity that makes them among the most ecologically and economically valuable coastal ecosystems:

**Fisheries production:** Approximately 90% of tropical commercial fish species depend on mangrove habitat at some point in their life cycle — as nursery grounds for juveniles, as feeding areas, or as refuge. The link between mangrove extent and nearshore fish productivity is one of the most robust relationships in coastal ecology.

**Storm surge and coastal protection:** Mangrove forests reduce wave height by 50–70% and storm surge by 50–75% depending on forest width and density. This provides measurable protection to coastal communities, reducing infrastructure damage and loss of life during cyclones and tsunamis. The 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami caused far less damage in villages behind intact mangroves than in adjacent deforested areas.

**Blue carbon storage:** Mangroves are the highest-density blue carbon ecosystem, storing carbon in above-ground biomass and in anaerobic peat sediments up to 3 metres deep. Estimated sequestration: approximately 4.4 tC/ha/year in fast-growing young forests. When cleared, this peat oxidises rapidly, releasing centuries of stored carbon.

**Coastal erosion prevention:** Mangrove root systems stabilise sediment, reduce shoreline erosion, and support accretion of land in areas of positive sediment budget.

**Biodiversity:** Mangroves support specialised communities including mudskippers, fiddler crabs, proboscis monkeys (Borneo), saltwater crocodiles, waterbirds, and reef fish species in adjacent waters.

## Site requirements for successful restoration

Successful mangrove restoration requires site conditions that match the species' ecological requirements. The most common cause of restoration failure is planting at unsuitable sites:

| Parameter | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **Tidal range** | 0.5–4 m; mangroves must experience regular inundation and drainage |
| **Salinity** | 5–35 ppt (most species); highly variable by species |
| **Sediment** | Soft, anaerobic; positive sediment budget (not net-eroding) |
| **Inundation frequency** | Species-specific; typically 30–70% of time inundated |
| **Wave energy** | Low to moderate; high-energy exposed shores require structures or are unsuitable |

Sites with impaired hydrology — blocked by roads, sea walls, or aquaculture bunds — often cannot sustain planted mangroves even if conditions otherwise appear suitable. Restoring tidal flow is the first intervention.

## Restoration approach

### 1. Community involvement (non-negotiable)

Mangroves sit at the interface of coastal livelihoods. Local fishing communities, crab collectors, and small-scale aquaculture operators depend on mangroves for income and food security. Top-down restoration imposed without community consent frequently fails through sabotage, grazing, or simple non-compliance. Co-management agreements that give communities a stake in the restored forest — access rights, carbon revenue sharing, co-governance — consistently outperform externally managed projects.

**The community question to answer first:** Do the people who live here support this restoration? If not, find out why before planting anything.

### 2. Hydrological restoration

Many degraded mangrove sites have impaired hydrology — roads, bunds, or drainage channels block tidal flow. Before planting, assess whether tidal flow can be restored. In many cases, simply removing an obstruction allows natural mangrove regeneration from surviving propagules and seed banks, with no planting required. Hydrological restoration alone is often sufficient and is always lower-cost than planting.

### 3. Replanting (when natural regeneration fails)

When the propagule supply is too low for natural regeneration, direct planting is appropriate:
- **Use local provenance seeds and seedlings** — species and genetic material from the same region as the target site; introduced species or inappropriate genetics reduce long-term resilience
- **Match species to tidal zone** — different mangrove species have narrow tolerance ranges for inundation frequency; planting the wrong species in the wrong zone is a primary cause of failure
- **Avoid monoculture** — natural mangroves are multi-species; monocultures are more vulnerable to pests, disease, and climate events
- **Collect propagules locally** — walk adjacent healthy mangrove stands and collect fallen propagules; avoids nursery cost and ensures local adaptation

### 4. Monitoring and adaptive management

Survival monitoring at 3, 6, 12, and 24 months is essential. Replanting of failed areas should be triggered early. Survival below 60% at 12 months should trigger a site-condition review before further investment.

## Timeline

| Milestone | Timeframe |
|---|---|
| Initial seedling establishment | 6–18 months |
| Canopy closure (basic shade structure) | 5–10 years |
| Full fisheries nursery function | 10–20 years |
| Full carbon sequestration function | 50+ years |
| Full peat development | Centuries |

## Common mistakes

| Mistake | Why it fails |
|---|---|
| Planting in the wrong tidal zone | Seedlings drown or desiccate |
| Using non-local species | Reduced adaptation; genetic dilution of wild populations |
| Monoculture planting | Low resilience; unnatural structure |
| Skipping hydrology assessment | Impaired tidal flow kills all plantings |
| No community engagement | Project undermined; maintenance fails |
| Planting at net-eroding sites | Sediment loss exposes roots; collapse within 5 years |
| Treating restoration as a one-time event | Requires multi-year monitoring and adaptive replanting |

## Financing pathways

- **REDD+ credits:** Under the UNFCCC framework, nations can claim credits for avoided deforestation. Mangrove conservation qualifies when combined with national accounting.
- **Blue carbon voluntary credits:** Verra VM0007 (REDD+) and VM0033 (tidal wetland restoration) provide methodology for generating tradeable carbon credits from mangrove conservation and restoration.
- **Government programs:** Indonesia has committed to restoring 600,000 ha of mangroves; Bangladesh, Vietnam, and Thailand have large national restoration programs.
- **Fisheries co-benefit financing:** Some programs tie restoration financing to fisheries productivity agreements with local fishing communities.

## OIS integration

Guardian agents for mangrove coast regions use Copernicus Marine Service data to assess site suitability:
- **Salinity:** CMEMS salinity fields at 0.25° resolution
- **Temperature:** SST and seasonal variability
- **Sediment dynamics:** Turbidity proxies from Sentinel-2 surface reflectance
- **Tidal range:** AVISO sea level and tidal prediction products

Combined with Protected Planet data and OBIS species occurrence records, the guardian can produce a site-suitability matrix for any proposed restoration site in a mangrove region.
