# Coral Gardening

## What it is

Coral gardening is the most widely deployed active reef restoration technique. It works in three stages: **fragment**, **grow**, **outplant**.

1. **Fragment:** Aquarists or trained divers collect small branches (5–10 cm) from healthy donor coral colonies. Fragmentation mimics natural breakage from storms — corals are adapted to it and regenerate quickly.
2. **Grow:** Fragments are attached to structures in nurseries — typically underwater PVC "coral tree" frames or mesh panels suspended in the water column, or in land-based flow-through tanks — where they grow in ambient reef conditions without competition from algae or predators.
3. **Outplant:** Once colonies reach 5–15 cm diameter and demonstrate stability, divers attach them to degraded reef substrate using marine epoxy or cement plugs. The outplanted colony continues growing in place.

The cycle from fragmentation to outplanting typically takes **12 months** under healthy nursery conditions.

## Nursery types

**In-situ floating nurseries (most common):** PVC tree structures or hanging lines suspended at 3–8 m depth, anchored to mooring buoys away from the reef. Fragments hang in well-flushed, warm, clear water with good light. Low-cost to establish; no land infrastructure needed; scales well. The Coral Restoration Foundation's coral tree design is the most widely replicated model.

**Land-based tank nurseries:** Recirculating or flow-through saltwater systems on land. Advantages: controlled temperature (can maintain optimal growth conditions), protection from storm damage, and ability to raise species too sensitive for in-situ nurseries. Disadvantages: high energy and infrastructure cost; corals must acclimate to open-water conditions before outplanting.

## Species used

Restoration programs focus on species that:
- Are structurally important (framework-building)
- Have declined most severely
- Can be propagated from fragments

Primary species in Florida and the Caribbean:
- *Acropora cervicornis* (staghorn coral) — fast-growing; branching; critical for structural complexity
- *Acropora palmata* (elkhorn coral) — historically dominant on shallow reef crests; severely depleted
- *Orbicella faveolata* (mountainous star coral) — massive; long-lived; reef framework
- *Porites* spp. (finger/mounding corals) — tolerant and hardy

In the Indo-Pacific (including Australia), *Acropora* and *Porites* species are also primary targets, alongside massive Faviidae corals.

## Survival and success rates

- **Year 1 survival:** 70–80% under well-managed conditions
- **Year 5 survival:** approximately 50% in stable sites
- Survival is highly site-dependent — sites with recurring thermal stress or poor water quality show lower survival rates regardless of nursery quality

Survival metrics alone do not capture restoration success. Full success requires:
- Colony survival (keeping outplants alive)
- Recruitment of wild larvae (wild corals settling near outplants — indicates ecosystem function returning)
- Return of fish species that depend on complex coral structure
- Net increase in live benthic coral cover over baseline

## Cost and scale

| Cost element | Range |
|---|---|
| Per colony (nursery growth + outplanting labor) | $15–25 |
| Full site program per year | $50,000–150,000 |
| Cost per m² of restored benthic cover | $200–800 (highly variable) |

**Scale achieved:** The Coral Restoration Foundation (Florida Keys, founded 2007) has outplanted over 175,000 coral colonies since inception — the largest coral restoration program in the world by volume. This represents a meaningful proof of scale, though the total area of reef functionally restored remains a small fraction of what has been lost.

## When to use coral gardening

Coral gardening is appropriate when:
- The reef has experienced bleaching or physical damage but retains conditions that support recovery
- Water quality is sufficient for outplanted colonies to survive (low sediment, low nutrient, no chronic thermal stress)
- The site is within the thermal tolerance margin — if sea surface temperatures frequently exceed bleaching thresholds, outplants will bleach as fast as they are planted
- Donor colonies of the target species exist nearby in healthy condition
- The restoration team can commit to multi-year monitoring and maintenance

## When NOT to use coral gardening

- Reefs with chronic, recurring thermal bleaching — outplants will not outlast the next heat event
- Sites with poor water quality driven by unresolved upstream land management (nutrient runoff, sedimentation) — until the source problem is fixed, restoration is planting into a degraded system
- Where wild coral recruitment is already occurring at healthy rates — active restoration may not add value if natural recovery is underway

## OIS integration

Guardian agents for coral reef regions (GBR Reef Guardian, Florida Reef Guardian) check the following before recommending a restoration window:
- NOAA Coral Reef Watch Degree Heating Weeks (DHW) — restoration is recommended only when DHW is below 2°C-weeks and forecast to remain low for 90+ days
- ERDDAP thermal anomaly data for site-specific sea surface temperature
- Protected Planet boundary data to confirm the site is within managed protection

The guardian outputs a "restoration window open/closed" status and flags if conditions at a target site are suitable for outplanting operations.
