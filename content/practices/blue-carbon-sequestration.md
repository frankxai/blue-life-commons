# Blue Carbon Sequestration

## What blue carbon is

Blue carbon is the carbon stored in marine and coastal ecosystems. The term is used primarily to describe carbon captured and stored in three coastal habitat types:

1. **Mangrove forests** — tropical and subtropical intertidal forests at land-ocean boundaries
2. **Seagrass meadows** — submerged flowering plants forming dense meadows in shallow coastal waters globally
3. **Salt marshes (tidal marshes)** — grass-dominated intertidal wetlands in temperate regions

These three ecosystems store carbon both in living biomass (above-ground vegetation) and in the sediment below them. The sediment carbon pool is the key differentiator: in coastal blue carbon ecosystems, reduced oxygen in waterlogged sediments slows decomposition dramatically, allowing carbon to accumulate in deep peat layers over thousands of years. Mangrove peat can exceed 3 metres depth.

## Why it matters: the carbon storage comparison

| Ecosystem | Carbon sequestration rate | Total carbon stored (global) |
|---|---|---|
| **Mangroves** | ~4.4 tC/ha/year | 3.14–6.4 Gt C (estimated) |
| **Seagrasses** | ~1.4 tC/ha/year | ~9.8 Gt C (estimated) |
| **Salt marshes** | ~2.1 tC/ha/year | ~0.87 Gt C (estimated) |
| Tropical forests (comparison) | ~2.6 tC/ha/year | — |

Coastal blue carbon ecosystems sequester carbon at rates **up to 50x higher per unit area** than tropical forests on a per-hectare basis, primarily because sediment carbon accumulates continuously rather than cycling back to the atmosphere through decomposition. The critical factor is that this carbon is only retained while the ecosystem is intact. When mangroves are cleared or seagrass beds destroyed, the sediment is exposed to oxygen and the stored carbon oxidises rapidly — releasing centuries of stored carbon back to the atmosphere within years.

## The three key ecosystems

### 1. Mangroves
~36 million hectares globally (declining); concentrated in tropical Asia, Africa, and the Americas. Mangroves store carbon in above-ground biomass (roots, trunks, canopy) and in deep anaerobic peat sediments. They also provide coastal protection, nursery habitat, and livelihood support for hundreds of millions of coastal people.

**Sequestration rate:** approximately 4.4 tC/ha/year in young, fast-growing forests; lower in mature forests.

### 2. Seagrasses
~177,000 km² globally (declining ~7%/year in recent decades). Seagrasses capture carbon primarily in their below-ground root and rhizome systems and in associated sediment. Their carbon storage per unit area is lower than mangroves but their global extent is larger.

**Sequestration rate:** approximately 1.4 tC/ha/year; sediment carbon pools estimated at 9.8 Gt C globally.

### 3. Salt marshes
~55,000 km² globally; heavily impacted in developed nations. Salt marshes sequester carbon in roots and sediment. They are among the most threatened coastal habitats, with major historical losses in North America, Europe, and East Asia.

**Sequestration rate:** approximately 2.1 tC/ha/year.

## The voluntary carbon market

Blue carbon has entered mainstream voluntary carbon markets. Verra's Verified Carbon Standard (VCS) VM0033 methodology provides a framework for generating carbon credits from tidal wetland and seagrass restoration. Typical credit prices range from **$8–40 per tonne of CO2** depending on project quality, co-benefits (biodiversity, community livelihoods), and market conditions.

For a project to generate saleable credits, it must demonstrate:
- **Additionality:** the carbon storage would not have occurred without the project
- **Permanence:** the carbon will remain stored for the project period (typically 20–100 years)
- **No leakage:** protecting or restoring habitat in one place doesn't drive equivalent destruction elsewhere
- **Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV):** rigorous carbon accounting through soil cores, biomass measurement, and sediment accretion monitoring, typically on 3–5 year cycles

## The leakage problem

The most significant integrity challenge in blue carbon is **leakage**: if mangroves are protected in one area through carbon finance, but pressure to clear mangroves for aquaculture or agriculture simply displaces to an unprotected area, the net carbon outcome may be zero or negative even as credits are issued. Additionality (would this forest have been cleared without the project?) and leakage (did protection here cause clearing elsewhere?) must be rigorously demonstrated.

This is particularly challenging in seagrass restoration, where the drivers of seagrass loss (coastal development, water quality degradation) are rarely within a single project's control.

## Monitoring requirements

Standard MRV (Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification) for blue carbon projects includes:
- **Soil cores** — sediment sampling at multiple depths to determine carbon density and accumulation rate
- **Above-ground biomass measurement** — stem density and diameter surveys
- **Sediment accretion measurement** — Surface Elevation Table (SET) measurements to track whether sediment is accreting or eroding
- **Remote sensing** — aerial or satellite imagery to track canopy extent over time

Typical monitoring cycles: major verification every 3–5 years; annual remote sensing updates.

## OIS integration

Guardian agents can assess the blue carbon value of proposed restoration sites using:
- Copernicus Marine Service data for site-level salinity, temperature, and bathymetry conditions
- OBIS occurrence records to characterise existing ecosystem composition
- Protected Planet data to assess whether the site is within a protected area (affects additionality analysis)
- Published regional carbon density data from the IUCN Blue Carbon Initiative to estimate potential sequestration from restoration

A guardian configured for a coastal wetland region can generate a site-suitability scorecard that includes estimated carbon sequestration value alongside biodiversity and coastal protection co-benefits.
