# Ocean Acidification: Chemistry, Consequences, and Coral's Fragile Future

The ocean has absorbed roughly 30% of all carbon dioxide emitted by human activity since the Industrial Revolution. That sounds like good news — the sea as a planetary buffer. The chemistry that follows is not.

## The Chemistry: What Acidification Actually Means

When CO₂ dissolves in seawater, it reacts with water molecules to form carbonic acid (H₂CO₃). Carbonic acid rapidly dissociates into bicarbonate ions (HCO₃⁻) and hydrogen ions (H⁺). It is those free hydrogen ions that lower the ocean's pH — making the water more acidic in the chemical sense, even though seawater remains slightly alkaline overall.

The pH scale is logarithmic, not linear. A change of 0.1 pH units represents a 26% increase in hydrogen ion concentration. Surface ocean pH has fallen from approximately 8.18 before industrialisation to 8.08 today — that 0.1-unit drop is the 26% figure cited in most summaries of ocean acidification. The current rate of change is approximately −0.0017 pH units per year, and the pace is accelerating as atmospheric CO₂ continues to rise. At current trajectories, surface ocean pH could reach 7.95 by 2100 — a shift with no parallel in the geologic record over the past 300 million years, certainly not one occurring on timescales that evolution can track.

## The Calcification Crisis: Shells Under Siege

The downstream consequence that matters most for marine ecosystems is what acidification does to calcium carbonate (CaCO₃). Organisms that build shells, skeletons, and tests — corals, oysters, mussels, sea urchins, and many planktonic species — depend on CaCO₃ in two mineral forms: calcite and the more soluble aragonite.

The key measurement is the aragonite saturation state (Ω arag). When Ω arag falls below 1.0, the water is undersaturated with respect to aragonite: shells dissolve faster than organisms can build them. Surface ocean aragonite saturation has already declined from a pre-industrial value of around 3.4 to approximately 2.8 today. But the Southern Ocean — the coldest, most CO₂-soluble ocean basin — is projected to reach aragonite undersaturation seasonally by the 2050s, and year-round in large areas thereafter.

## Pteropods: The Canary in the Carbonate Sea

Perhaps the most vivid demonstration of acidification's biological costs comes from pteropods — free-swimming sea snails sometimes called sea butterflies. The species *Limacina helicina* is a tiny organism, rarely larger than a few millimetres, but it is foundational to Arctic and sub-Arctic food webs.

Dissolving pteropod shells were observed in the Beaufort Sea as early as 2012. When researchers compared pteropods from pre-industrial shell material (preserved in museum collections) with contemporary specimens, they found measurable dissolution and pitting in the more recent shells — physical evidence that the organisms are already operating outside their evolutionary tolerance.

The food web consequence scales upward sharply. Pteropods constitute roughly 50% of the diet of pink salmon (*Oncorhynchus gorbuscha*) in Alaskan waters during certain life stages. A collapse in pteropod populations does not stay in the plankton layer — it propagates through salmon, seabirds, and the Indigenous and commercial fisheries that depend on them.

## Coral Calcification: Decades of Documented Decline

Reef-building corals deposit their calcium carbonate skeletons through a metabolically expensive process called calcification. As the aragonite saturation state declines and hydrogen ion concentrations rise, corals must work harder to maintain the internal chemistry required for skeletal growth — and frequently fail to do so at historical rates.

Long-term data from the Australian Institute of Marine Science (AIMS) and studies at sites including Palau show that coral calcification rates have declined by 13–15% per decade since 1970. This is not a projected future loss; it is a measured retrospective trend drawn from coral cores that preserve the annual growth record like tree rings. Slower calcification means thinner, more fragile skeletons — less able to withstand storm damage, less able to recover from bleaching events.

The compound pressure matters. Acidification does not act alone. Elevated sea surface temperatures trigger bleaching; storm damage removes skeletal structure; acidification slows the recovery calcification that rebuilds it. The combination drives reefs toward a state where erosion outpaces growth — a net dissolving reef — in some locations before mid-century.

## Food Security: The Oyster Industry as a Case Study

The economic implications of acidification are no longer theoretical. The Pacific oyster (*Crassostrea gigas*) aquaculture industry in Oregon, USA experienced documented larval failures in hatcheries between 2006 and 2010. Hatcheries drawing seawater from near-shore upwelling zones found that larvae were failing to calcify properly, with mortality rates that threatened commercial viability.

Investigation linked the failures to episodic upwelling of corrosive deep water — water with aragonite saturation states below 1.0 — reaching the surface. This deep water had absorbed CO₂ from the atmosphere decades earlier in polar regions and had been transported subsurface along the eastern Pacific continental shelf. The Oregon crisis was, in a sense, the bill arriving for emissions that predated many of the hatchery operators.

Shellfish aquaculture globally is valued at approximately $3.8 billion per year. Oysters, clams, mussels, and scallops are disproportionately important in coastal economies and food systems across Asia, Europe, and North America. The Oregon example is an early-warning case, not an isolated incident.

## What Can Actually Be Done

Only one intervention addresses the root cause of ocean acidification: reducing atmospheric CO₂ concentrations. Carbon dioxide is the feedstock of carbonic acid in seawater. As long as atmospheric CO₂ rises, ocean pH will continue to fall, and the chemistry will not reverse on any human-relevant timescale even if emissions stopped today — the ocean continues equilibrating with the atmosphere for centuries.

Local interventions can buffer specific sites without solving the global problem. Reducing nutrient runoff from agriculture decreases the frequency of hypoxic events and limits localised acidification from organic matter decomposition. Protecting marine protected areas (MPAs) builds ecosystem resilience by keeping coral communities as healthy as possible, improving their capacity to withstand chemical stress. Seaweed and kelp aquaculture operations consume dissolved CO₂ during photosynthesis, creating local pockets of higher pH — an approach with promise for specific sheltered sites though not scalable to ocean-wide effect.

The honest summary: ocean acidification is a physical and chemical consequence of atmospheric CO₂ accumulation, measurable today, accelerating, and already causing demonstrable biological harm at the base of multiple food webs. The only leverage that changes the trajectory is the same leverage that governs the broader climate response.
