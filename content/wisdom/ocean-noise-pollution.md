# Ocean Noise Pollution — The Invisible Threat

Before you can understand what we are doing to the ocean with noise, you need to understand what sound means to marine life. The ocean is not a silent world. It is a world that runs on sound.

---

## A Sound-Mediated World

Water conducts sound approximately **4.3 times faster** than air and over vastly greater distances. A blue whale's low-frequency call can travel **hundreds of kilometers** in the right acoustic conditions. Sperm whales use focused sound pulses — biosonar — to navigate in complete darkness at depths of 2,000 meters and locate prey they cannot see. Fish communicate during spawning aggregations using sounds that coordinate collective behavior. Shrimp colonies produce crackling soundscapes that guide larval reef fish to suitable settlement sites.

The ocean acoustic environment is an information system. Species have evolved over millions of years to extract critical signals from it: predators at range, mates during breeding season, prey in darkness, habitat in open water. This system depends on signal-to-noise ratios that allow biological signals to be distinguished from background. Human activity is systematically degrading those ratios.

---

## Sources of Human Noise

**Commercial shipping** is the most pervasive source of underwater noise pollution. Container ships, tankers, and bulk carriers generate continuous broadband noise from their propellers (cavitation) and engines, concentrated at low frequencies (10–1,000 Hz) — the same frequency range used by baleen whales for long-distance communication.

Global shipping traffic has grown dramatically since 1950. Measurements from hydrophones in the North Pacific show that **ambient low-frequency ocean noise has increased by approximately 32-fold since 1950** — a 15 decibel increase. Where a blue whale's call might once have been detectable by another blue whale at 1,000 km, the effective range has been compressed to tens of kilometers in heavily trafficked shipping lanes.

**Seismic air guns** used in offshore oil and gas exploration generate some of the loudest human-made sounds in the ocean — **230+ decibels at source** — and fire every 10–12 seconds for days or weeks at a time. These surveys blanket enormous ocean areas with intense, repetitive sound pulses. Marine mammals, fish, and invertebrates within hundreds of kilometers can experience acute acoustic trauma, behavioral disruption, and chronic stress. Air gun surveys are conducted across vast stretches of the deep ocean annually.

**Military sonar** — particularly medium-frequency active sonar (MFAS) used for submarine detection — generates pulses of 190–230 decibels. Multiple **mass stranding events** involving beaked whales have been directly linked to naval sonar exercises. The mechanism: sonar exposure causes acute behavioral responses (panic surfacing, diving behavior changes) that lead to decompression-like injuries and death. The US Navy has acknowledged the connection and reached legal settlements over stranding events linked to specific exercises.

**Construction** — offshore wind foundation installation involves pile-driving, which generates percussive impulses detectable at extreme ranges. As offshore wind development scales globally, the acoustic footprint of construction periods is increasingly significant, particularly in the North Sea and US Atlantic seaboard.

**Recreational vessels** contribute at smaller geographic scales but can have significant impacts in areas with high vessel density and critical marine mammal habitat — particularly in passages used by endangered whale populations.

---

## Impacts on Cetaceans

For dolphins, porpoises, and toothed whales that rely on echolocation (biosonar), noise is not merely an annoyance — it is a direct interference with their primary sensory system. **Southern Resident Killer Whales (SRKWs)**, listed as endangered in both Canada and the US, exemplify the cascading effects of underwater noise.

SRKWs hunt almost exclusively by acoustic detection. They emit high-frequency clicks, listen for the returning echo, and locate Chinook salmon — their primary prey — in turbid or deep water where visual hunting is impossible. Vessel noise — particularly from commercial shipping and whale watch vessels in Haro Strait and the Strait of Juan de Fuca — **masks this echolocation at critical frequencies**.

Research published by Rolland et al. (2012) demonstrated the direct physiological cost: when ambient vessel noise decreased (following the post-9/11 reduction in shipping traffic), fecal glucocorticoid metabolites (stress hormones) in SRKW samples fell measurably. The relationship between vessel noise and chronic physiological stress was not inferred — it was measured in hormone concentrations in whale feces.

**Noise budget modeling** for SRKW critical habitat calculates the total acoustic energy available for echolocation after background vessel noise is subtracted. When vessel traffic is high, the remaining acoustic "budget" for foraging may be insufficient for successful hunting. Combined with declining Chinook salmon availability — the prey side of the equation — noise is contributing to a foraging success crisis in an already critically endangered population.

For baleen whales that communicate over long distances, the compression of communication range by shipping noise has population-level consequences: reduced ability to locate mates, coordinate group behavior, and maintain social bonds across the acoustic landscape.

---

## Impacts on Fish and Invertebrates

Fish are not passive recipients of acoustic energy. Many species produce and detect sound for spawning coordination, predator avoidance, and larval settlement.

**Spawning aggregations** of many commercially important fish species depend on acoustic signaling to coordinate reproductive timing. Noise pollution at the wrong frequencies and intensities can disrupt these aggregations, with downstream impacts on reproductive success and recruitment.

**Larval settlement** in coral reef systems depends on acoustic cues. Healthy reefs produce characteristic soundscapes — fish calls, snapping shrimp — that attract settling larvae to suitable habitat. Degraded reefs produce degraded soundscapes. Larvae seeking settlement habitat in noisy, degraded environments may settle in suboptimal locations or fail to settle at all.

**Invertebrates** including cephalopods (squid and octopus) show documented physiological damage from low-frequency sound exposure — hair cell damage in statocysts (their balance and acceleration sensors) following seismic survey exposure. The full scope of invertebrate vulnerability to anthropogenic noise is an active area of research.

---

## Solutions

Unlike ocean warming or acidification — which require global emissions reductions — ocean noise pollution is amenable to targeted, faster interventions.

**Vessel speed reduction** is the single most effective near-term measure. Slowing vessels from 14 knots to 11 knots can reduce underwater radiated noise by approximately **40% (6 decibels)**. Several shipping lanes adjacent to critical marine mammal habitat have implemented voluntary or mandatory speed reduction programs — the Ports of Los Angeles and Long Beach, the Canadian Pacific whales initiative in the St. Lawrence Seaway, and the SRKW interim sanctuary approach in the Salish Sea.

**Propeller redesign** — specifically cavitation-optimized propellers — can reduce noise generation at source without speed penalties. The technology exists; adoption is a matter of regulatory incentive and shipping industry economics.

**IMO guidelines** on underwater noise from commercial shipping have existed since 2014 but remain voluntary. Elevating them to mandatory standards would be one of the fastest-acting interventions available for ocean acoustic health.

**Seismic survey alternatives** — including ocean-bottom seismometer networks that do not require active sound sources — are technically viable for many applications. Regulatory pressure to shift toward quieter survey methods is mounting in several jurisdictions.

**Temporal and spatial management** — establishing seasonal or geographic restrictions on high-intensity sound sources in critical habitat — is already practiced in some areas and could be significantly expanded.

---

## The Connection to Southern Resident Killer Whales

For anyone following the SRKW population specifically: vessel noise is consistently ranked as the **second-greatest threat** to the population, after prey availability. The three threats — prey, noise, and toxins — are interactive: a nutritionally stressed whale has less physiological capacity to cope with chronic acoustic stress; higher noise levels require more energy expenditure to hunt successfully, which reduces caloric balance when prey is already scarce.

Solutions for SRKWs require addressing all three simultaneously. Vessel noise reduction is the fastest-acting and most directly controllable of the three.
