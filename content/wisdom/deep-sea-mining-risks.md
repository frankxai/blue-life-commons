# Deep-Sea Mining: Nodule Ecology, Fragility, and Why Caution Matters

The deep ocean floor is the largest ecosystem on Earth, and it is largely intact. That may be about to change. Commercial interest in seabed mineral extraction has intensified sharply since 2010, driven by demand for battery metals — cobalt, nickel, manganese, copper — and by projections that terrestrial reserves will struggle to meet requirements for the energy transition. The case for deep-sea mining rests on those supply pressures. The case against rests on the biology.

## What Deep-Sea Mining Targets

Three categories of mineral deposit attract commercial attention, each found in different parts of the ocean and each requiring different extraction methods.

**Polymetallic nodules** are potato-shaped concretions that litter the surface of abyssal plains at depths of 4,000–6,000 metres. They form around a nucleus — a shark tooth, a fragment of bone — accreting layers of manganese, nickel, cobalt, and copper from the surrounding seawater. The Clarion-Clipperton Zone (CCZ) in the central Pacific Ocean is the most commercially targeted nodule field, covering an area roughly the size of the continental United States.

**Seafloor massive sulphide deposits** form at hydrothermal vents — underwater hot springs where mineral-rich superheated water erupts from the ocean floor and precipitates deposits of gold, silver, copper, zinc, and lead. These deposits are concentrated, relatively small in area, and located in some of the most biologically unique environments on the planet.

**Cobalt-rich ferromanganese crusts** coat the flanks and summits of seamounts, the extinct underwater volcanoes that dot the Pacific and Atlantic. Seamounts are biodiversity hotspots — hard substrate in the deep ocean is rare, and seamount fauna includes dense aggregations of cold-water corals, sponges, and fish.

## The Growth Rate Problem

The commercial case for nodule mining often describes the resources as vast. The ecological case begins with how long they took to form. Polymetallic nodules grow at a rate of 1–2 centimetres per million years. This is not a typo. One centimetre per million years — among the slowest geological processes known.

Collector vehicles envisioned for nodule mining would scrape the uppermost 5–10 centimetres of sediment across swaths of abyssal plain that collectively span hundreds of thousands of square kilometres. At 1–2 cm per million years, the recovery timescale for nodule regrowth is measured in tens of millions of years. For all practical human purposes, this is a non-renewable extraction.

## The DISCOL Experiment: Thirty Years of Evidence

In 1989, German researchers conducted the Disturbance and Recolonization (DISCOL) experiment in the Peru Basin of the South Pacific — one of the most valuable long-term datasets in deep-sea ecology. A specially designed plough harrow was dragged across 10.8 km² of abyssal seafloor, mimicking the disturbance a collector vehicle would cause. The site was then monitored over the following three decades.

The results were unambiguous. A 2019 study examining the DISCOL site 30 years later found that fauna characteristic of undisturbed nodule fields — polychaete worms, sponges, holothurians (sea cucumbers), and xenophyophores (giant single-celled organisms) — were still largely absent from ploughed tracks. Microbial communities had partially recovered, but the macrofaunal community that makes nodule fields ecologically functional had not. The sediment plume generated by the disturbance dispersed at least 300 kilometres from the disturbance site, blanketing a vastly larger area than was directly ploughed.

The DISCOL experiment tested a much smaller disturbance than commercial mining would create, in a single location, with no repeat operations. Commercial nodule mining in the CCZ would operate continuously across millions of square kilometres over decades. The DISCOL data provides no basis for confidence that ecosystems would recover within any timeframe relevant to human decision-making.

## Biodiversity: What We Don't Know We'd Lose

Abyssal plains were long assumed to be biological deserts — featureless, cold, dark, and sparsely inhabited. That picture was dismantled by systematic sampling beginning in the 1980s and accelerating through the 2000s. A single square kilometre of nodule field can harbour more than 1,000 species of invertebrates, the overwhelming majority of them undescribed by science. These include novel isopods, polychaetes, nematodes, foraminifera, and organisms with no close known relatives.

Hydrothermal vent fauna are ecologically distinct from the surrounding deep sea. Rather than depending on photosynthesis as the base of their food web, vent communities are fuelled by chemosynthetic bacteria that derive energy from hydrogen sulphide. The result is ecosystems with endemism rates above 95% — species found at one vent system and nowhere else on Earth. Mining the seafloor massive sulphide deposits that form at active or recently extinct vents would permanently destroy these communities, many of which have never been catalogued.

## The Carbon Store Argument

A 2021 study published in *Science* added a carbon dimension to the mining risk calculus. Nodule fields and the sediment beneath them store organic carbon in concentrations that, while low per unit volume, aggregate to significant quantities across the scale of abyssal plains: an estimated 1.8 Gt (gigatonnes) of carbon in organisms and sediment within the CCZ alone. Mining operations — both the direct physical disturbance and the sediment plumes they generate — transport this carbon upward out of the deep ocean, where it had been effectively sequestered, into mid-water and surface layers where it can be remineralised and re-released to the atmosphere. The study found that plume dispersion carries this carbon well outside the direct mining area, making the effect difficult to contain or offset.

## Commercial Pressure and Regulatory Context

As of 2025, the International Seabed Authority (ISA) — the United Nations body responsible for governing mineral extraction in international waters — had issued exploration contracts covering approximately 1.3 million km² of the Pacific, Atlantic, and Indian Oceans. The CCZ alone accounts for roughly 900,000 km² of licensed exploration area. Several contractors moved toward applications for exploitation licences in the early 2020s, triggering the "two-year rule" in ISA regulations and forcing a renegotiation of the governance framework.

The global moratorium movement has gained substantial momentum. As of 2026, 24 countries had either called for a moratorium on deep-sea mining or expressed support for a precautionary pause pending adequate environmental baseline data and regulatory frameworks. The IUCN — the International Union for Conservation of Nature — has recommended no-go zones for mining around hydrothermal vents and seamounts and has called for a moratorium on nodule mining in the absence of evidence that significant harm can be avoided.

The High Seas Treaty — formally the Agreement on Marine Biological Diversity of Areas Beyond National Jurisdiction (BBNJ), adopted in 2023 and entered into force in 2025 — creates new legal architecture for environmental impact assessment and marine protected areas in the high seas. Its implementation will directly shape what additional protections can be placed on the areas most targeted by mining interests.

## The Consumer Connection

For individuals and organisations engaged in ocean advocacy, deep-sea mining connects directly to the electric vehicle battery supply chain. The primary metals of commercial interest — cobalt and nickel especially — are also the metals used in lithium-ion battery cathodes. Consumer and investor pressure on automakers and battery producers to require independent ESG verification of deep-sea mineral supply chains is one of the more tractable points of leverage outside international governance processes.

The precautionary case is straightforward: we do not have baseline biodiversity surveys adequate to assess what would be lost, we have evidence from DISCOL that recovery does not occur on human timescales, and we are still discovering the ecological functions these systems perform. The burden of proof should rest with those proposing extraction, not with those urging caution.
