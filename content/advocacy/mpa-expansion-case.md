# Making the Case for MPA Expansion: Economic, Ecological, and Social Arguments

Marine Protected Areas are the primary policy instrument for conserving marine biodiversity, but the advocacy case for them is often made on ecological grounds alone. That argument, while scientifically sound, leaves persuasive territory on the table. Policymakers, finance ministries, and fishing industry stakeholders respond to different evidence hierarchies. This resource builds the complete argument across ecological, economic, and social dimensions.

## The Ecological Case

The ecological case for MPAs rests on the most thoroughly replicated finding in marine conservation science: protection works. Inside fully protected no-take MPAs — areas where all extractive activities are prohibited — fish biomass, species diversity, and organism size all increase measurably relative to unprotected comparison sites.

A meta-analysis of 124 studies published globally found that fish density inside fully protected MPAs averages 670% higher than in adjacent unprotected areas (Lester et al. 2009, meta-analysis of 124 studies). This is not a ceiling effect in the most depleted regions — the uplift is observed across baselines ranging from near-pristine to heavily exploited.

The foundational spillover study by Roberts et al. (2001), published in *Science*, documented that fish biomass in areas adjacent to newly established MPAs increased by 15–30% within five years of protection. This spillover effect — the movement of adults and larvae from inside the protected area into adjacent fished zones — is the mechanism that makes MPAs not merely a conservation tool but a fisheries management tool. In ecological terms, MPAs function as production reservoirs: they maintain the breeding populations whose output sustains the surrounding fishery.

## The Economic Case

The economic case for MPA expansion has been substantially strengthened by Sala et al. (2021), published in *Nature*, which provides the most comprehensive cost-benefit analysis of ocean protection at global scale.

The headline finding: protecting 30% of the global ocean would generate economic benefits of approximately $542 billion through fisheries recovery alone over a defined evaluation period, relative to a no-protection baseline. The benefit-cost ratio for well-designed MPAs — accounting for enforcement, monitoring, and transition costs — is approximately 3.3:1 per unit area within a 10-year horizon. The economics are most favourable for MPAs established in heavily fished areas with documented stock depletion, where the recovery potential is largest.

Tourism provides a separate economic argument that operates independently of fisheries. The Great Barrier Reef Marine Park — 344,400 km², established 1975 — generates approximately AUD $6.4 billion in direct and indirect economic contribution to the Australian economy per year, supporting around 64,000 full-time equivalent jobs. This figure, from Queensland government economic analysis, rests primarily on reef tourism and recreational fishing, both of which depend on the ecological integrity that protection maintains.

The blue economy framing extends further still. Coastal tourism and recreation globally — much of it dependent on healthy marine ecosystems — is valued in the hundreds of billions of dollars annually. The destruction of a reef system does not simply reduce biodiversity; it eliminates the physical substrate that reef tourism depends on, with permanent economic consequences for coastal communities.

## The Climate Resilience Case

A dimension of MPA effectiveness that has gained increasing scientific attention is the relationship between protection status and climate resilience.

Analyses of bleaching responses across the Great Barrier Reef and Indo-Pacific reefs more broadly find that fully and highly protected reefs bleach approximately 30% less severely than adjacent unprotected reefs during comparable thermal stress events. The proposed mechanisms include healthier fish communities that support algae-grazing functions and maintain cleaner substrates for coral recovery, as well as the absence of physical disturbance from fishing gear that compounds thermal stress damage.

The blue carbon argument applies most directly to coastal habitats — mangroves, seagrass meadows, and tidal marshes — rather than offshore coral reefs, but it reinforces the case for comprehensive coastal MPA networks. Protected seagrass beds store significantly more carbon per unit area than disturbed equivalents; the same applies to mangrove fringe systems. Degradation by trawling, boat anchoring, or coastal runoff releases that stored carbon. Protection retains it.

## The 30×30 Context

The Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework, adopted at COP15 in December 2022 and signed by 196 parties, established Target 3: protect 30% of land, freshwater, and ocean by 2030. The 30×30 target gives MPA expansion advocacy a specific, internationally agreed reference frame that connects individual project proposals to a global commitment.

As of 2026, approximately 8% of the global ocean is under some form of protection, up from 0.7% in 2000. Progress over the past two decades has been real. The pace required for the next four years is dramatically greater: meeting 30×30 requires placing approximately 6 million km² per year under effective protection through to 2030 — roughly the size of Australia, added every year.

The quality of that protection matters as much as the area. Approximately 42% of existing MPAs contain no measurable management activity or enforcement — what researchers term "paper parks." Advocacy for MPA expansion should explicitly incorporate governance and enforcement requirements to avoid contributing to the paper parks problem.

## Practical Advocacy Asks

When translating the ecological and economic evidence into policy asks, the most defensible and actionable requests take this form:

**No-take core zones with managed-use buffer zones.** The scientific evidence for spillover and biomass recovery is strongest for fully protected no-take areas. Managed-use buffer zones allow sustainable fishing and recreation while ensuring the ecological core is intact. This structure has the broadest stakeholder acceptability and the strongest evidence base.

**WDPA registration and IUCN categorisation.** Internationally recognised MPAs must be registered in the World Database on Protected Areas (WDPA) and assigned an IUCN management category. This is both a governance baseline and a prerequisite for counting toward 30×30 national targets.

**Enforcement planning before designation.** An MPA without an enforcement plan is a paper park. The policy ask should include a defined enforcement budget, monitoring protocol, and governance structure as conditions of designation, not afterthoughts.

**Community co-management as a success predictor.** The most robust predictor of long-term MPA effectiveness in the literature is not enforcement intensity or legal status in isolation — it is local community co-management. MPAs designed with fishing communities as governance partners achieve better compliance, lower enforcement costs, and better ecological outcomes than top-down exclusion models. This finding, replicated across dozens of case studies in the Pacific Islands, Caribbean, and East Africa, should anchor the social dimension of any MPA expansion proposal.
