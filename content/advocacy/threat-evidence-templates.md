# Threat Evidence Templates: Data-Backed Narratives for Ocean Advocacy

Ocean advocacy fails most often not at the passion level but at the evidence level. Funders, policymakers, and media all apply increasing scrutiny to environmental claims, and the field has accumulated enough poorly sourced, inflated, or outdated statistics to have inoculated decision-makers against numbers they cannot verify. The goal of this resource is to give advocates templates for evidence-grounded narratives that will hold up under scrutiny.

## Why Data-Grounded Narratives Win

Funding committees, parliamentary committees, and editorial standards editors all operate within an implicit evidence hierarchy. Understanding that hierarchy lets you pitch at the right tier and cite at the right level of authority.

**Tier 1 — Peer-reviewed literature:** Studies published in indexed journals with documented methodology and statistical analysis. These carry the most weight with scientific reviewers and are the expected standard for policy submissions. When citing Tier 1 sources, include author, year, journal, and DOI wherever possible.

**Tier 2 — Government and intergovernmental agency data:** NOAA, FAO, IPCC working group reports, IUCN assessments, UN Environment Programme publications. These are compiled from primary research and are generally acceptable to policymakers. They are more accessible than academic papers and more defensible than NGO reports.

**Tier 3 — NGO reports, advocacy publications, and news articles:** Useful for framing and context but rarely sufficient as standalone evidence for funding applications or policy submissions. Tier 3 sources should be used to illustrate, not to substantiate.

Funders at foundation level increasingly require outcome evidence, not impact stories. A narrative that says "our MPA project could protect a key reef system" is weaker than "our MPA project is designed to replicate the documented 15–30% fisheries spillover effect measured in comparable fully-protected MPAs (Lester et al. 2009)." The evidence structure does the persuasion work that adjectives cannot.

## Template 1 — Coral Bleaching Threat Narrative

**Narrative structure:**
1. Baseline state with date — what the system looked like before documented degradation began
2. Documented decline — quantified, cited, with observation period stated
3. Causal linkage — the mechanism connecting your threat to the outcome, with the strongest available evidence
4. Projected outcome — modelled futures with scenario and source cited
5. Specific ask — the concrete intervention that the evidence supports

**Example opening hook:** "The Great Barrier Reef has experienced five mass bleaching events since 1998 — in 2002, 2016, 2017, 2020, and 2022. Before 1998, no mass bleaching event had been recorded in any historical or palaeoclimate record of the reef system."

**Causal linkage formulation:** Degree Heating Weeks (DHW) — the metric that captures accumulated thermal stress above bleaching thresholds — provide the quantitative bridge between temperature anomalies and bleaching outcomes. NOAA Coral Reef Watch DHW data shows a documented correlation between DHW values above 8°C-weeks and mass bleaching events with high mortality. Cite the specific DHW values for each bleaching event you reference.

**What to avoid:** Do not claim a "collapse" unless you can document population-level extinction or functional loss. Do not use "unprecedented" without a comparison baseline and time period. Do not conflate bleaching with mortality — many bleached corals recover if thermal stress is not repeated or prolonged.

## Template 2 — Fisheries Pressure Narrative

Global wild-capture fisheries production peaked at approximately 86 million tonnes in 1996 (FAO). Since then, the wild catch has plateaued or declined, while aquaculture has filled growing demand. This transition has been uneven: many high-value species remain under severe pressure, and a significant fraction of reported catch is Illegal, Unreported, and Unregulated (IUU).

**IUU framing:** IUU fishing costs the global economy an estimated $26 billion per year in lost fish value and undermines the stock management systems that legal fisheries depend on (FAO). When making the case for MPA expansion or fisheries enforcement funding, the IUU cost figure provides a direct economic return argument.

**MPA expansion ask structure:** Frame the ask in terms of documented spillover rather than conservation abstraction. Reference the proportion of a specific fishery's key habitat that falls within existing MPA coverage, quantify the gap, and cite the economic value of potential spillover recovery. The Sala et al. (2021) *Nature* paper provides the most comprehensive benefit modelling for ocean protection: protecting 30% of the ocean generates an estimated $542 billion in fisheries recovery benefits over a defined period.

## Template 3 — Species Population Decline Arc

The IUCN Red List provides the most widely accepted framework for documenting species status. When building a population decline narrative, the following rules prevent common errors.

Use the IUCN population trend data directly: Decreasing, Stable, Increasing, or Unknown. Do not construct your own trend claims from raw data unless you have the statistical methodology to support them. Always cite the assessment year — IUCN assessments are periodically updated, and an outdated assessment can undermine a submission. Check the current assessment date at iucnredlist.org before citing.

Avoid projections without citation. "Species X could go extinct within 20 years" is a claim that requires a specific published projection study with defined assumptions. Without that citation, the claim is an assertion. With it, the same sentence becomes evidence.

## Template 4 — Plastics Threat Narrative

Marine plastic pollution is one of the most widely recognised ocean threats, and one of the most frequently cited with inaccurate or unverified numbers. The most defensible current estimates: 8–12 million tonnes of plastic enter the ocean per year (Jambeck et al. 2015 *Science* for the lower bound; subsequent studies revise upward). The economic cost of marine debris to fisheries and tourism is estimated at $13 billion per year (UNEP). Ghost gear — abandoned, lost, or discarded fishing equipment — accounts for approximately 640,000 tonnes of ocean plastic per year (FAO/UNEP Ghost Gear Initiative).

**What to avoid:** The "ocean plastic island twice the size of Texas" framing, while evocative, misrepresents how the Great Pacific Garbage Patch works. It is a diffuse accumulation zone, not a solid mass, and its visual representation as a discrete island is not accurate. Using that framing invites fact-check rebuttals that undermine adjacent claims.

## What Undermines Advocacy Narratives

Four patterns consistently damage credibility with expert reviewers:

**Cherry-picked dates** — selecting a measurement year that maximises the apparent trend, without disclosing the full data range. Any reviewer can request the underlying data.

**Unverifiable claims** — numbers sourced from secondary summaries that cannot be traced to a primary study. Always trace to the primary source and check that the number you are repeating is what the study actually found.

**Conflating association with causation** — "fish populations declined at the same time temperatures rose" is an observation. "Temperature rise caused the fish population decline" requires a causal mechanism with documented evidence.

**Outdated IUCN assessments without date-flagging** — using a 2015 IUCN assessment to describe a species' current status when the most recent assessment is 2023 is a straightforward error that a reviewer will catch.
