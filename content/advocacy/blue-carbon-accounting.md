# Blue Carbon Accounting: Methodology, Monitoring, and Market Integrity

Blue carbon is carbon stored in coastal marine ecosystems. The term entered mainstream conservation finance in the early 2010s and, like much of the voluntary carbon market, has undergone a period of rapid growth followed by a harder scrutiny of whether the claims hold up. This resource explains the science, the dominant methodology frameworks, and the integrity questions that advocates and project developers need to understand.

## What Blue Carbon Is

Three coastal ecosystem types are the primary subjects of blue carbon accounting: mangrove forests, tidal marshes (also called salt marshes or coastal wetlands), and seagrass meadows. Together, these ecosystems cover approximately 0.2% of the global ocean surface — a tiny fraction of total ocean area — but they store a disproportionate share of ocean-associated organic carbon.

The key characteristic that makes these ecosystems carbon-significant is anaerobic decomposition. When organic matter (dead leaves, root material, algal debris) accumulates in waterlogged soils, the absence of oxygen slows microbial decomposition dramatically. Carbon that would oxidise rapidly in a terrestrial forest instead becomes embedded in sediment layers that can persist for millennia. Radiocarbon dating of blue carbon sediment cores has identified organic carbon deposited thousands of years ago still intact in undisturbed mangrove and seagrass systems. This millennial-scale sequestration is the primary distinction between blue carbon and most terrestrial carbon sinks, where carbon turns over on timescales of decades.

The global blue carbon map, documented by Howard et al. (2017) and subsequent analyses, estimates that coastal blue carbon ecosystems store the equivalent of several hundred gigatonnes of CO₂ in their soils and biomass — dwarfing their above-ground appearance would suggest.

## The Three Habitats: Carbon Density Compared

The carbon storage density of the three habitats differs substantially, reflecting differences in productivity, sedimentation rates, and soil depth.

**Mangroves** have the highest carbon density of any blue carbon ecosystem, with estimates typically in the range of 800–1,000 tonnes CO₂ equivalent per hectare of total ecosystem carbon (above-ground biomass plus soil). This high density reflects both the productivity of mangrove trees and the deep anaerobic soils that accumulate beneath them, in some systems reaching several metres depth. Mangroves are also the blue carbon ecosystem under greatest immediate threat: global mangrove extent has declined by approximately 35% since 1980 due to aquaculture conversion, coastal development, and wood harvesting.

**Tidal marshes** have estimated carbon densities of approximately 800–900 tonnes CO₂e per hectare, comparable to mangroves in well-developed marsh systems. They are distributed primarily in temperate latitudes and have experienced substantial historical loss through drainage, reclamation for agriculture, and coastal development.

**Seagrass meadows** have lower per-hectare carbon density — estimates typically in the range of 200–400 tonnes CO₂e per hectare — but are globally extensive (approximately 300,000 km²) and are declining at approximately 7% per year globally, meaning they represent both a significant existing carbon stock and a rapidly accelerating source of emissions as degradation continues.

## Methodology Standards

The voluntary carbon market uses standardised methodologies to calculate and verify carbon credits generated by blue carbon projects. Two frameworks dominate.

**Verra's Verified Carbon Standard VM0033** (Methodology for Tidal Wetland and Seagrass Restoration) is the most widely used methodology for blue carbon project development. VM0033 establishes the technical requirements for: establishing a counterfactual baseline (what would have happened without the project); demonstrating additionality (that the project's conservation or restoration activity goes beyond what would have occurred anyway); calculating permanence (accounting for the risk that stored carbon will be re-released); accounting for leakage (displacement of deforestation or degradation to outside the project boundary); and ongoing monitoring and verification by approved third-party auditors.

**The IUCN Blue Carbon Initiative methodology** provides a framework more oriented toward national accounting and policy rather than project-level credit generation. It is increasingly referenced in countries developing blue carbon components of their Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) under the Paris Agreement.

The process for a project developer typically follows these steps: conduct a baseline assessment of current ecosystem carbon stocks and trends; demonstrate additionality against a business-as-usual scenario; submit to a Verra-approved validation/verification body (VVB); generate Verified Carbon Units (VCUs); list on the Verra registry; and undergo periodic monitoring and re-verification.

## Market Integrity Challenges

The voluntary carbon market experienced a credibility crisis in 2023–2024 that affected blue carbon projects alongside forest and soil carbon projects. Several high-profile investigations — notably a joint journalistic analysis of the Verra REDD+ programme — found that a significant proportion of credits issued did not represent the emissions reductions claimed. Blue carbon projects faced specific integrity challenges.

**Permanence risk:** Sea-level rise poses a long-term existential risk to coastal mangrove and marsh systems. A project that generates carbon credits from mangrove conservation but that is projected to be inundated by 2070 has a permanence problem that standard buffer pool accounting may not adequately address.

**Leakage:** If a project protects a mangrove stand from aquaculture conversion within its boundary but aquaculture operators simply move to an adjacent unprotected area, the project has generated paper credits while achieving no net conservation. Leakage accounting methodologies exist but remain contested in implementation.

**Double-counting:** Countries are increasingly including blue carbon in their nationally submitted NDCs under the Paris Agreement. If a government counts a mangrove restoration project's carbon sequestration toward its own national emissions inventory while a project developer also sells credits from the same project on the voluntary market, the same carbon reduction is claimed twice. The accounting frameworks for preventing this under Article 6 of the Paris Agreement are still being operationalised.

High-integrity blue carbon credits are distinguished by: third-party validation and verification by accredited bodies; permanence buffer pools sized to the actual project risk profile; explicit leakage deduction in the credit calculation; registry transparency; and no double-counting with host country NDC submissions.

## How NGOs Can Use Blue Carbon

For marine conservation organisations, the blue carbon mechanism offers a potential revenue stream for coastal habitat restoration projects. The financial model for a mangrove project is illustrative: a 200-hectare mangrove restoration project in a suitable climate might generate 10,000 to 50,000 tonnes of CO₂e per year in verified credits. At market prices in the range of $15–50 per tonne (the range seen for high-integrity voluntary credits in 2023–2025), this generates $150,000 to $2.5 million annually in carbon revenue, potentially covering monitoring costs and local co-management employment while the restored ecosystem provides fisheries, coastal protection, and biodiversity benefits.

For organisations evaluating blue carbon as a funding mechanism, the key steps are: engage a Verra-approved methodology expert early in the project design phase; commission an independent baseline carbon stock assessment; design monitoring and community governance arrangements before seeking funding; and select a verification body accredited under Verra or Gold Standard rather than a self-certified auditor.

The IUCN Blue Carbon Initiative and the Seagrass and Coastal Carbon programme (SEACAP) provide technical guidance and support networks for organisations entering the blue carbon space for the first time. Plan Vivo offers an alternative certification standard that is particularly well-suited to small-scale community-based projects and places greater emphasis on co-benefits and community governance than the Verra framework.

The integrity questions raised in 2023–2024 have not invalidated blue carbon as a concept or as a mechanism. They have established a higher bar for project design and verification — a bar that organisations entering the space now, with awareness of those questions, are better positioned to clear than earlier projects were.
