# 30×30 Progress Tracker: Where We Are, Where the Gaps Are

The 30×30 target is the most ambitious ocean protection commitment in history. Understanding where progress stands — and where the critical gaps are — is essential for anyone working on marine conservation policy, funding advocacy, or public communication about ocean protection.

## What 30×30 Is

Target 3 of the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework, adopted at the 15th Conference of the Parties to the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD COP15) in December 2022, commits 196 signatory parties to effectively conserving and managing at least 30% of the world's land areas, inland waters, and coastal and marine areas by 2030.

The specific language matters for advocacy: the target is not simply "designated" but "effectively conserved and managed." This distinction is load-bearing. It means that paper parks — MPAs with no enforcement, no management plan, and no measurable ecological outcomes — should not count toward the target under a strict interpretation of the Framework text. The Framework also specifies that protected areas should be "ecologically representative and well-connected," which rules out approaches that hit 30% numerically by protecting only low-conflict, low-biodiversity deep water areas while leaving critical coastal and pelagic habitats unprotected.

## Where We Are: Current Ocean Protection Status

As of 2026, approximately 8.1% of the global ocean falls within some form of designated Marine Protected Area. This represents substantial progress from 0.7% in 2000, but the gap between 8.1% and 30% is not primarily a measurement problem — it is a governance and political will problem.

Of the 8.1% that is nominally protected:

- Only approximately 2.7% is classified as fully or highly protected (IUCN categories I and II, or equivalent no-take designations)
- Approximately 42% of existing MPAs have no documented management activity — they are registered in the World Database on Protected Areas but have no evidence of active governance, monitoring, or enforcement
- A significant proportion of recent MPA designations are in remote, deep-water areas with low biodiversity significance and minimal conflict with extractive industries, inflating the area statistics without proportional conservation gain

Meeting 30×30 for the ocean by 2030 requires adding approximately 6 million km² of genuinely protected area per year for four consecutive years. For context, the entire Mediterranean Sea covers approximately 2.5 million km². The required annual addition is more than double the Mediterranean.

## Which Biomes Are Most Under-Protected

The distribution of current protection is highly skewed toward shallow coastal areas and remote low-conflict zones. Three biome categories stand out as critically under-protected relative to their ecological significance.

**High seas beyond national jurisdiction:** Waters outside the Exclusive Economic Zones of coastal states — approximately 61% of the ocean surface — are currently only 1.2% protected. Until the BBNJ Agreement entered into force in 2025, there was no legal instrument that could create MPAs in these areas. The high seas also contain a disproportionate share of the ocean's most ecologically important habitats: deep pelagic zones, seamount chains, and mid-ocean ridge systems.

**Deep-sea habitats:** Abyssal plains, hydrothermal vent fields, seamounts, and cold-water coral ecosystems are estimated to be less than 0.1% protected. These ecosystems are also the primary targets for deep-sea mining interests, creating a direct tension between commercial pressure and conservation need.

**Polar waters:** Both Arctic and Antarctic marine areas have seen improving MPA coverage, but total protection remains below 5% of polar waters. The CCAMLR (Commission for the Conservation of Antarctic Marine Living Resources) process for establishing large-scale Antarctic MPAs has faced repeated blocking by China and Russia in annual meetings. The East Antarctic and Weddell Sea MPA proposals, which would collectively protect over 2 million km² of the Southern Ocean, have been on the CCAMLR agenda for over a decade.

## Regional Leaders and Laggards

Progress toward 30×30 varies dramatically by nation and region.

Among leaders, the European Union has committed to 30×30 within EU waters and is implementing the EU Biodiversity Strategy for 2030, with interim milestones and monitoring frameworks. The Cook Islands' Marae Moana — a 1.9 million km² ocean park established 2017 and expanded since — represents one of the most ecologically significant individual MPA designations in the world. Palau's National Marine Sanctuary and the UK's network of Overseas Territories MPAs (including the Chagos Archipelago, though its governance status remained under review in 2026) contribute substantially to global statistics.

Among laggards, several major fishing nations lack binding domestic MPA targets or have designated MPAs that permit industrial fishing and mining. The United States established a 30×30 national goal under the Biden administration's "America the Beautiful" initiative, but the programme's implementation status came under review in 2025–2026, creating uncertainty about domestic follow-through on international commitments.

## The Key Instruments for Filling the Gaps

**The BBNJ Agreement:** Formally titled the Agreement under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea on the Conservation and Sustainable Use of Marine Biological Diversity of Areas Beyond National Jurisdiction, this treaty entered into force in 2025 after achieving the required 60 ratifications. It is the first international legal instrument capable of creating MPAs in the high seas beyond national EEZs — the ocean area that represents the largest gap in current protection coverage. Implementation now depends on domestic ratification by remaining parties and the establishment of operational governance bodies.

**CCAMLR Antarctic Protections:** The Commission for the Conservation of Antarctic Marine Living Resources has jurisdiction over the Southern Ocean and manages a small number of existing MPAs including the South Georgia and South Orkney Islands South MPA. Unlocking the large proposed MPAs (East Antarctic, Weddell Sea) requires consensus among all CCAMLR parties. Diplomatic engagement with blocking parties is a specific advocacy target for Antarctic conservation organisations.

**The Sargasso Sea Alliance:** The Sargasso Sea — uniquely important as the only sea bounded by ocean currents rather than land, and the only breeding habitat for European and American eels — sits entirely in international waters. The Sargasso Sea Commission and Hamilton Declaration provide a governance framework but not binding protection. Its inclusion in high-seas MPA proposals under BBNJ is a priority campaign for several NGOs.

## Citizen Accountability Tools

Advocates and concerned citizens have access to several publicly available tools for tracking 30×30 progress at national and global levels.

The **Protected Planet Dashboard** at protectedplanet.net, maintained by UNEP-WCMC and IUCN, provides country-by-country statistics on protected area coverage, updated as new designations are registered. It is the most authoritative public source for the global statistics cited in this document.

The **Marine Conservation Institute's MPA Atlas** provides more granular analysis of MPA quality, distinguishing between fully protected, highly protected, and weakly or not protected designations at a resolution that the WDPA global statistics obscure.

To check your country's 30×30 progress and identify advocacy asks: review your country's National Biodiversity Strategy and Action Plan (NBSAP), submitted to the CBD following COP15. Find your country's current marine protected area percentage on protectedplanet.net. Identify whether existing MPAs have documented management effectiveness. Then ask your elected representatives: what is our domestic 30×30 plan, what is the enforcement budget for existing MPAs, and have we ratified the BBNJ Agreement?
