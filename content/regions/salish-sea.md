# Salish Sea

## Overview

The Salish Sea is a system of interconnected inland marine waters shared by the United States and Canada, encompassing Puget Sound (Washington State), the Strait of Georgia (British Columbia), and the Strait of Juan de Fuca. Its total marine surface area is approximately 17,000 km². The surrounding watershed is one of the most densely populated coastal regions in the Pacific Northwest — Seattle, Vancouver, and Victoria all sit on its shores, with approximately 9 million people living in the drainage basin.

Despite heavy industrialisation, the Salish Sea retains extraordinary biodiversity: 37 species of marine mammals, 247 fish species, and approximately 3,000 total species, making it one of the most biodiverse temperate marine regions on Earth. It is also one of the most heavily monitored and regulated, driven largely by the precarious status of its most visible inhabitants.

## Flagship species: Southern Resident Killer Whales

Southern Resident Killer Whales (SRKWs) are the defining conservation challenge of the Salish Sea. This fish-eating ecotype forms three family pods:

- **J pod** — year-round Salish Sea residents
- **K pod** — seasonal; summers in the Salish Sea
- **L pod** — the largest pod; ranges more widely along the coast

The population hovers near historic lows. As of the most recent Center for Whale Research census (~2025), the population numbers approximately 73 individuals — a near-historic low. The population is listed as Endangered under the U.S. Endangered Species Act (since 2005) and under Canada's Species at Risk Act. Critical habitat is designated in inland Washington State waters (US, 2006) and was expanded in 2021 to include coastal waters from Washington through California.

**Why SRKWs are in trouble — three interlocking threats (NOAA framework):**

1. **Prey depletion (Chinook salmon):** SRKWs are highly specialised; 80%+ of their diet is Chinook salmon (*Oncorhynchus tshawytscha*). Snake River and other Columbia River system Chinook runs have collapsed following dam construction and habitat degradation. When Chinook abundance is low, whales lose body condition, females fail to carry pregnancies to term, and calf survival drops. This is the primary driver of population decline.

2. **Vessel noise and disturbance:** The Salish Sea carries approximately 1,700 large commercial vessels per year plus 11,000+ recreational vessels in or near designated critical habitat. Underwater noise from vessels at close range masks the echolocation SRKWs use to detect prey — directly interfering with their ability to feed. Vessel disturbance at distance also displaces foraging behaviour.

3. **Contaminants:** PCBs (polychlorinated biphenyls), PBDEs (flame retardants), and other persistent organic pollutants bioaccumulate in SRKW tissues through the food chain. PCB levels in SRKWs are among the highest recorded for cetaceans. Contaminants suppress immune function and reproductive success. PCBs were banned in the 1970s but persist in Salish Sea sediments and continue cycling through the food web.

## Additional marine mammal diversity

The Salish Sea supports:
- Transient (Bigg's) killer whales — mammal hunters; a recovering population; not endangered
- Pacific harbor porpoise (*Phocoena phocoena*)
- Steller's sea lions (*Eumetopias jubatus*) — seasonally abundant
- California sea lions (*Zalophus californianus*) — winter visitors
- Harbor seals (*Phoca vitulina*) — year-round residents; abundant
- Humpback and gray whales — seasonal passage

## Current ecological state

The SRKW population trajectory is not improving despite two decades of regulatory intervention. Births and deaths have been roughly balanced, with some years showing net loss. The 2021 expansion of critical habitat was the most significant regulatory action in years; the 2024–2025 period has seen continued advocacy for dam removal on the Snake River as the highest-leverage single intervention for salmon recovery.

The Trans Mountain Pipeline expansion (completed 2024) increased tanker traffic in Burrard Inlet and portions of the Salish Sea, adding to underwater noise and oil spill risk concerns.

## Protected area and vessel rules

**US — Washington State law (effective 1 January 2025):** Vessels must maintain a minimum distance of **1,000 yards (914 m)** from Southern Resident killer whales in Washington State waters. Additional provisions require power-down below a speed threshold when SRKWs approach within a wider zone.

**US — Federal rule:** NOAA Fisheries' federal regulation requires vessels to stay at least **200 yards** from all killer whales and out of their path in US waters.

**Canada — Fisheries and Oceans Canada:** Interim orders set mandatory approach distances for SRKWs in southern BC waters, updated seasonally. The current season's interim order should be consulted for exact figures — distances have been progressively extended in recent years.

**Critical habitat:** US critical habitat covers inland Washington State waters plus coastal Pacific waters from Washington through California (94,990 km² US designation). Canada's critical habitat designation covers 3.5M km².

**Note on location precision:** Guardian instances monitoring SRKW locations coarsen reported positions to the nearest 10 km grid cell to avoid publishing real-time locations that could increase vessel-approach traffic to feeding or resting groups.

## Conservation programs

- **NOAA Southern Resident Killer Whale Recovery Plan** — the primary federal management framework; updated periodically; identifies Chinook, noise, and contaminants as the three co-equal stressors.
- **Center for Whale Research (Friday Harbor, WA)** — annual photo-ID census since 1976; the authoritative population count.
- **Be Whale Wise** (US–Canada joint program) — public education on vessel approach guidelines.
- **Salish Sea Marine Survival Project** — multi-agency research program investigating juvenile salmon marine survival.
- **Pacific Salmon Treaty** (US–Canada) — bilateral management of shared salmon runs.
- **Snake River dam removal** — evaluation of removing four lower Snake River dams to restore Chinook migration route; considered the highest-leverage single recovery action.

## Guardian coverage (OIS)

The Salish Sea Orca Guardian monitors:
- OBIS occurrence records for SRKW sightings (coarsened 10 km grid)
- IUCN Red List status for *Orcinus orca* and associated prey species
- WoRMS taxonomic records
- Center for Whale Research census data (annual)
- NOAA vessel traffic datasets

Location precision is deliberately coarsened in all public outputs to avoid directing vessel traffic toward active foraging groups.

## What NGOs and advocates can do

- **Advocate for Snake River dam removal** — restoring free-flowing Chinook migration is the single highest-leverage intervention for SRKW prey availability.
- **Support vessel slowdown zones and speed-reduction programs** in SRKW critical habitat during summer months (when whales concentrate for Chinook feeding).
- **Fund the Center for Whale Research annual census** — 50 years of continuous monitoring is an irreplaceable scientific asset.
- **Push for PCB cleanup** in Puget Sound and Columbia River sediments — a long-horizon but necessary contaminant intervention.
- **Promote shore-based whale watching** as an alternative to vessel-based viewing — zero noise impact, and often better sightlines from headlands.
