# For Educators & Students

You need ocean material that is accurate, current, and honest about uncertainty — and most of what's available is stuck in PDFs or pitched at the wrong level. The commons gives you reviewed, source-cited species pages and region briefings you can teach from directly, plus a clean path to turn them into lessons.

## What the system gives you

- **Reviewed species pages** — each with an `## At a glance` table (scientific name, IUCN status with date, population trend, range), identification, ecology, conservation status, and a sourced "how you can help." See the six in [`CATALOG.md`](../../CATALOG.md), e.g. [`content/species/turtles/green-turtle.md`](../../content/species/turtles/green-turtle.md).
- **Region briefings** — place-scoped overviews (Monterey Bay, Ningaloo Coast, Wadden Sea) that anchor a lesson in a real, local ecosystem.
- **A model of intellectual honesty** — the green turtle page teaches a rare and valuable thing: a 2025 IUCN downlisting that is *not* "recovered everywhere," with some subpopulations still threatened and the species still on CITES Appendix I. That nuance is the lesson.
- **A `lesson` artifact type** — author a lesson pack grounded in a species page, so your teaching material itself becomes reviewed, citable commons knowledge.
- **`marine-mcp` for the classroom** — let students ask an AI assistant grounded questions and watch it cite (or refuse). The refusal-when-unreviewed behavior is itself a media-literacy lesson.

## How to use it today

1. **Pick a species page as your lesson core** — open the green turtle page. The `## At a glance` table is a ready-made handout; the conservation section is a discussion prompt about why "Least Concern" doesn't mean "safe."
2. **Pair it with a region briefing** — connect the species to a place students can picture (e.g. green turtles → a tropical region briefing), so ecology stops being abstract.
3. **Teach source literacy with the citations** — every claim links to a Tier 1–2 source ([`SOURCES.md`](../../SOURCES.md)). Have students follow one citation to its IUCN or CITES origin and check the access date.
4. **Demo grounded AI** — if you can install [`marine-mcp`](https://github.com/frankxai/marine-mcp), ask it about a species and show how it returns sources — or a typed refusal when a page isn't review-approved. "Grounded or silent" is exactly what students should demand from any AI.
5. **Author a `lesson`** — distill a species page into a lesson plan, cite the page's `id`, and contribute it back.

## The rules that apply to you

- **Cite as the page cites.** If you extend a claim beyond the page, it needs its own Tier 1–2 source. Report figures as the authority states them.
- **Don't add approach guidance.** Species pages deliberately *don't* tell you how to approach an animal — that's the job of reviewed observation guides and field missions with welfare rules. Keep that separation in your materials.
- **No anthropomorphism as fact** ([`ETHICS.md`](../../ETHICS.md)). Teach behavior from cited research, not "what the whale feels."
- **Respect location sensitivity** — never publish or assign precise nesting beaches or haul-out sites; the pages keep to regional granularity for a reason.

## Your first contribution

**Turn one species page into a `lesson` artifact.** You already do this work for your classroom — formalizing it as a reviewed lesson (citing the source page's `id`, keeping every claim sourced) gives the commons something it's actively missing and gives your students a published artifact they helped shape. Open an `artifact-request` issue in [`.github/ISSUE_TEMPLATE/`](../../.github/ISSUE_TEMPLATE/) and use the species page's sources as your spine.
