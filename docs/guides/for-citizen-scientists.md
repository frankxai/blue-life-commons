# For Citizen Scientists & Volunteers

You give your time to the sea — counting seals, logging sightings, walking a beach on a schedule. The commons gives you field missions you can run today with the welfare rules built in, and a way to feed your verified observations back into a living body of knowledge with your credit on it.

## What the system gives you

- **Ready-to-run field missions** — citizen-science missions with everything you need: minimum distances, disengagement rules ("when to leave"), signs of disturbance, seasonal sensitivities, gear lists, and a "do no harm" section that overrides everything else. See [`CATALOG.md`](../../CATALOG.md): Harbor Seal Beach Monitoring (California) and Ethical Whale Shark Observation at Ningaloo.
- **Species pages to prepare with** — so you know what you're looking at and what its conservation context is before you go out.
- **The platforms to log to** — missions and species pages point you to recognized citizen-science platforms (e.g. the iNaturalist dataset card in `content/research/`) where your observations become usable data.
- **A real contribution path** — your reviewed mission or observation becomes a commons artifact with your contributor credit attached, flowing to the website, map, and impact ledger.

## How to use it today

1. **Pick a mission near you** — open the Harbor Seal Beach Monitoring mission. Read it end to end *before* you go: the distances, the disengagement triggers, the disturbance signs, the season.
2. **Prepare with the species page** — read the matching species page so you can identify confidently and report accurately.
3. **Run the mission by the rules** — keep the minimum distance, watch for disturbance signs, and leave the moment a "when to leave" trigger fires. The "do no harm" section beats your data-collection goal, always.
4. **Log your observations** — record them on a recognized platform (e.g. iNaturalist) so they become structured, citable data rather than a note in your phone.
5. **Contribute back** — clean field observations clearly labeled as first-person ("On this trip we observed…") are welcome, and a reviewed field mission you can run well is a genuine public good.

## The rules that apply to you

- **Follow the mission's welfare rules exactly** — distances and disengagement aren't suggestions ([`ETHICS.md`](../../ETHICS.md)). When local rules differ, the strictest applies.
- **First-person field observations** are clearly labeled as such and don't need a citation — but any *fact* you state (status, population, regulation) does ([`SOURCES.md`](../../SOURCES.md)).
- **No precise locations of vulnerable animals** — don't post the exact haul-out or nest, and watch the combination attack (a coarse location + season + a landmark in your photo can reconstruct a site).
- **No anthropomorphism as fact** — log behavior as observed, not as feelings interpreted.
- **Welfare over the count.** If your presence is disturbing an animal, the welfare interest wins — leave, even if it costs you the data point ([`WELFARE.md`](../../WELFARE.md)).

## Your first contribution

**Run an existing field mission and log your observations to a recognized platform — then propose one improvement.** That improvement (a clearer disengagement trigger, an updated season note, a disturbance sign you witnessed) is your first contribution, and it makes the mission better for every volunteer after you. Open a `field-mission` issue in [`.github/ISSUE_TEMPLATE/`](../../.github/ISSUE_TEMPLATE/) — or, if you want to author a new mission, use [`marine-agent-skills`](https://github.com/frankxai/marine-agent-skills) `/field-mission`, which builds the welfare sections in for you, then `/ethics-check` before the PR.
