# For Eco-Tourism Operators & Travelers

The difference between wildlife tourism that protects animals and tourism that harms them is discipline: distance, timing, and knowing when to leave. The commons gives you ethics-first field missions and region briefings so a trip is built on welfare science, not on getting the best photo.

## What the system gives you

- **Field missions with mandatory welfare rules** — every mission includes minimum approach distances, disengagement rules ("when to leave"), signs of disturbance for the target species, seasonal sensitivities, and local regulations. See the two live in [`CATALOG.md`](../../CATALOG.md): Harbor Seal Beach Monitoring and *Ethical Whale Shark Observation at Ningaloo (with a licensed operator)*.
- **Region briefings** — what lives where, what's protected, and what's in season for the coasts you operate on or travel to (Monterey Bay, Ningaloo, Wadden Sea).
- **The disturbance-budget frame** — the science that a per-actor rule ("stay 100 m back") can't stop the *fiftieth* boat at 100 m that flushes the haul-out. Harm is cumulative ([`WELFARE.md`](../../WELFARE.md)). Good operators manage their share of the total pressure, not just their own compliance.
- **Operator-selection discipline** — the species pages explicitly steer toward operators that keep their distance and don't handle animals.

## How to use it today

1. **Read the mission for your activity** — open the Ningaloo whale-shark mission. Note it's framed *with a licensed operator*, with disengagement rules and seasonal limits. That framing is the standard, not an optional extra.
2. **Build your trip from the welfare rules** — lift the minimum distances, the "when to leave" triggers, and the disturbance signs into your briefing for guests. Make the "do no harm" section override everything else, exactly as the mission does.
3. **Check the region briefing for season** — breeding, pupping, nesting, and molting windows are when disturbance costs the most. Schedule around them.
4. **Vet operators against the rules** — choose and recommend operators that hold distance, don't bait or chase, don't handle wildlife, and operate under documented permits where any closer interaction occurs.
5. **Contribute what you learned** — a clean, reviewed field mission for a trip you run well is a genuine public good and a credibility marker for your business.

## The rules that apply to you

- **Distance first.** All observation guidance specifies minimum approach distances; when regulations differ, cite the *strictest* applicable standard ([`ETHICS.md`](../../ETHICS.md)).
- **No interaction promotion.** Don't encourage touching, feeding, baiting, chasing, surrounding, or swim-with programs for wild animals unless under documented scientific/regulatory permits — and even then, *describe* rather than promote.
- **No precise locations of vulnerable populations** — no exact haul-out sites, nesting beaches, or aggregation coordinates. Regional granularity only. Watch the combination attack: a coarse location plus a season plus a landmark in your photo must not reconstruct an exact site.
- **Welfare over content value.** If guidance could plausibly disturb wildlife, it doesn't ship — and shouldn't be in your trip either.
- **Legal compliance is the floor, not the ceiling.** Cite the applicable rules and exceed them where welfare science suggests.

## Your first contribution

**Author a field mission for one trip you run to a high standard.** Use [`marine-agent-skills`](https://github.com/frankxai/marine-agent-skills) `/field-mission`, which builds in the mandatory welfare and disengagement sections, then `/ethics-check` (blocking) before you PR — or open a `field-mission` issue in [`.github/ISSUE_TEMPLATE/`](../../.github/ISSUE_TEMPLATE/). A reviewed mission with your operator's name on it is both a contribution to the sea and a trust signal to your guests.
